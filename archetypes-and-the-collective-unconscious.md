## The Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious

by C. G. Jung

*Collected Works Volume 9, Part I. Translated by R. F. C. Hull. Bollingen Series XX, Princeton, 2nd edition 1968.*

*Notes I took while reading this book to further my own learning. If you enjoy these notes, please [buy the book](https://www.amazon.co.uk/Archetypes-Collective-Unconscious-Collected-Works/dp/0691018332).*

*Published review: [stephanierebecca.com/books/archetypes-and-the-collective-unconscious](https://stephanierebecca.com/books/archetypes-and-the-collective-unconscious/)*

### Part I: Theoretical Foundations

#### Essay 1: Archetypes of the Collective Unconscious

- The unconscious has two layers. The *personal unconscious* is the upper layer: forgotten or repressed personal content. Freud's whole conception sits here. The *collective unconscious* is the deeper layer, *inborn, universal, identical in all individuals*, a common psychic substrate. [¶3]
- The contents of the personal unconscious are *feeling-toned complexes*. The contents of the collective unconscious are *archetypes*. [¶4]
- The term "archetype" goes back through Philo Judaeus (*imago Dei*), Irenaeus ("the creator copied them from archetypes outside himself"), the *Corpus Hermeticum* (archetypal light), Dionysius the Areopagite (immaterial Archetypes), and Augustine's *ideae principales*. It is the explanatory paraphrase of Plato's εἶδος. [¶5]
- The archetype is **not** the inherited image. It is the inherited *predisposition* to form the image. It is "an unconscious content that is altered by becoming conscious and by being perceived," taking its colour from the consciousness in which it appears. [¶6]
- Myth, fairytale, esoteric teaching, dogma are *culturally elaborated* archetypes: they preserve the form but lose some of the direct unconscious immediacy of dream and vision. [¶6]
- The mythologised processes of nature (sun's path, seasons, lunar phases) are not allegories of objective events. They are *projections* of the inner psychic drama onto outer phenomena. [¶7]
- "The stars of thine own fate lie in thy breast." (Seni to Wallenstein, ¶9) Astrology survives because the projection has not been withdrawn.
- **The Brother Klaus case [¶12–21] is the load-bearing example.** Nicholas of Flüe (15th-century Swiss mystic) had a vision so terrible his face was permanently disfigured. He spent years assimilating it via a German devotional booklet, eventually rendering it as a Trinity mandala. Jung's reading: the dogmatic image was the only available container; without it, the unmediated archetype would have destroyed him.
- **"Dogma takes the place of the collective unconscious by formulating its contents on a grand scale."** [¶21] The Catholic life is psychologically unaware of these problems because they have been channelled into ritual and symbolism.
- The Reformation's iconoclasm broke the protective wall of sacred images. Protestantism's history is one of chronic symbol-loss. The modern Protestant is "cast out into a state of defencelessness." [¶22–24]
- When archetypes lose their containers, the energy doesn't disappear. It floods into political and social systems. "The vacuum gets filled with absurd political and social ideas, which one and all are distinguished by their spiritual bleakness." [¶28]
- The recovery is *not* by importing Eastern symbols. We should "stoutly avow our spiritual poverty" rather than dress in foreign clothes. [¶28]
- *Water* is the canonical dream-symbol of the unconscious. To meet the unconscious one must descend, and the descent meets the *shadow* first. [¶34–43]
- **The shadow is the disowned personality.** "Whoever looks into the mirror of the water will see first of all his own face." Confronting it is "the first test of courage on the inner way." [¶43–44]
- *Persona* = the mask worn for the world. The shadow lives behind the mask. [¶43]
- The mirror "does not flatter, it faithfully shows whatever looks into it." Most people prefer projection (locating the shadow outside themselves) to the meeting. [¶43]
- "The collective unconscious is anything but an incapsulated personal system; it is sheer objectivity, as wide as the world and open to all the world." [¶46]
- *Pleroma* = the dissolved oceanic state in which the ego loses itself. Primitive man fears it because consciousness "wobbles on its feet" near it. Rite and dogma exist as "dams and walls" against this danger. [¶47]
- Modern politico-social delusional systems should be read "not causally, as necessary consequences of external conditions, but as decisions precipitated by the collective unconscious." [¶49]
- *Anima* is introduced via the dream-image of the nixie (water-being, half-fish, half-woman). She is the feminine archetype in the man's unconscious — sometimes mermaid, sometimes succubus, sometimes Helen of Troy, sometimes the Queen of Heaven. [¶52–53]
- The anima is not a moral category. She "wants life, she wants both good and bad." She is *life itself* as opposed to *meaning*. [¶59]
- "If the encounter with the shadow is the *apprentice-piece* in the individual's development, then that with the anima is the *master-piece*." [¶61]
- For the son, the anima is hidden first in the dominating power of the mother. Failure to detach produces lifelong "sentimental attachment that seriously impairs the fate of the adult." [¶61]
- The Freudian reduction of all neurosis to sexuality "confuses cause and effect." Sexual disturbance is a *symptom* of a maladaptation of consciousness, not its cause. [¶61]
- Behind the anima's chaotic capriciousness lies *the archetype of meaning* — what Jung sometimes calls the *Wise Old Man*. She is the archetype of life; he is the archetype of meaning. Together they form the syzygy. [¶66–67]
- The Wise Old Man appears in dream as the magician, the teacher, the elder, the *psychopompos*. In Nietzsche he is Zarathustra. In Goethe he is Faust's Mephistopheles-as-guide. In Hermes Trismegistus he is the master-teacher of the hermetic tradition. [¶74–79]
- Three personality-archetypes are *directly experienced in personified form*: shadow, anima, wise old man. Plus a different class: *archetypes of transformation* — typical situations, places, ways and means that symbolise the transformation itself. [¶80]
- "The symbolic process is an experience in images and of images. Its development usually shows an *enantiodromian* structure" (i.e., things turning into their opposites). [¶82]
- Possession by an archetype is the failure mode: "the patient will want to change himself into a woman through self-castration" (Schreber case). The pathological element "does not lie in the existence of these ideas, but in the dissociation of consciousness that can no longer control the unconscious." [¶82–83]
- *Individuation* = the synthetic process of integrating unconscious contents into consciousness. "A life in which the individual becomes what he always was." [¶84]

#### Essay 2: The Concept of the Collective Unconscious

- Four-part structure: (1) definition, (2) psychological meaning, (3) method of proof, (4) example. [¶87]
- **Definition.** "The collective unconscious is a part of the psyche which can be negatively distinguished from a personal unconscious by the fact that it does not, like the latter, owe its existence to personal experience." Its contents have *never been conscious* and were not individually acquired. They are *hereditary*. [¶88]
- Archetypes are the same thing other fields have already named: Lévy-Bruhl's *représentations collectives*, Hubert and Mauss's "categories of the imagination," Bastian's "elementary thoughts," Plato's εἶδος. Jung's contribution is recognising they are *psychic forms*, not just cultural ones. [¶89]
- **The empirical thesis:** "there exists a second psychic system of a collective, universal, and impersonal nature which is identical in all individuals... It consists of pre-existent forms, the archetypes, which can only become conscious secondarily." [¶90]
- Archetypes are "the unconscious images of *the instincts themselves*" — they are patterns of instinctual behaviour. The hypothesis of the collective unconscious is no bolder than the hypothesis of instincts. [¶91]
- **The Leonardo example.** Freud read St Anne with the Virgin and Christ-child as Leonardo's projection of his two biological mothers. Jung's counter: the *dual mother* motif (human + divine descent) is universal — Heracles, Pharaoh, Christ's baptism, godparents at modern baptisms all encode it. Leonardo was expressing what "countless millions of people before and after him have believed." [¶93–95]
- **Therapeutic implication.** In a patient with mother-complex, the personal interpretation ("you had two mothers") may be factually correct *and* therapeutically wrong. The actual cause is reactivation of the dual-mother archetype, "regardless of whether he had one mother or two." [¶96]
- Reducing everything to personal causes is medically dangerous: it denies the *constellated archetype* that is the real driver. [¶97]
- **The political diagnosis (writing in the 1930s).** Mass mobilisation around archaic symbols (Roman fasces, Roman salute, swastika replacing the cross) is exactly what archetype-activation looks like at scale. "There is no lunacy people under the domination of an archetype will not fall a prey to." [¶98]
- "There are as many archetypes as there are typical situations in life." Archetypes are *form without content* until a corresponding situation activates them. Then they "produce a compulsiveness that gains its way against all reason and will." [¶99]
- **Three sources of evidence** for archetypes: (1) dreams (involuntary, undistorted by conscious purpose); (2) *active imagination* — deliberate concentration on a fragment until associative context emerges; (3) the delusions of paranoid psychotics, trance fantasies, dreams of children aged 3–5. [¶100–103]
- Active imagination is *not* Freudian free association. It is sustained attention to one image, letting its context unfold. [¶101]
- **The Solar Phallus Man.** Around 1906 Jung observed a paranoid schizophrenic clerk who saw the sun's penis hanging like a tube and producing the wind by moving with his head. In 1910 Jung came across Dieterich's publication of a Mithraic ritual papyrus (Greek, Alexandrian) with the same image: "you will see hanging down from the disc of the sun something that looks like a tube... the origin of the ministering wind." The same image also appears in medieval paintings of Mary's fructification, where a tube descends from God's throne carrying the dove. [¶104–109]
- The patient could not have known the papyrus (published four years after his vision), the medieval painting (none in Zurich), or the Mithraic tradition. The image is *autochthonous*. This is Jung's clearest single piece of empirical evidence. [¶110]

#### Essay 3: Concerning the Archetypes, with Special Reference to the Anima Concept

- The anima is "a purely empirical concept, whose sole purpose is to give a name to a group of related or analogous psychic phenomena." Analogous to the zoologist's "arthropods" — a name for what is observed, not a metaphysical claim. [¶114]
- One cannot speak about the anima without knowing "the universal distribution and significance of the *syzygy motif*" — the male-female pair of deities found everywhere in primitive religion, mythology, and later philosophy. [¶115]
- *Syzygy* literally means "yoked pair." The yang-yin pair of classical Chinese philosophy is one example. Christianity removed the feminine half of the Trinity around the 4th century as heretical; the syzygy still shows up in mystics' visions (Brother Klaus saw God as both Father and Mother). [¶120, ¶131]
- **Projection is unconscious.** "The projection ceases the moment it becomes conscious." Therefore "the image of the parents is the very one that could be projected least, because it is too conscious" — yet experience shows parents *are* the most projected-upon images. The implication: there is something *beyond the personal parents* in the parental imagos. [¶121–122]
- That additional content is the *religious idea* attached to the parents. "It is more bearable for many people to admit their sexual fantasies than to confess that their analyst is a saviour." [¶123–124]
- "A man without a dominating *représentation collective* would be a thoroughly abnormal phenomenon." Atheism, materialism, communism, existentialism are all *variants of belief*, not escape from it. "No one can escape the prejudice of being human." [¶129]
- **The Brother Klaus syzygy.** A 15th-century Swiss peasant with no Gnostic education saw God as a majestic Father *and* a majestic Mother — directly contradicting a thousand years of Trinity orthodoxy. The archetype reasserted itself "spontaneously, independently of tradition." [¶131]
- Similar cases: Guillaume de Digulleville's monastic vision of the King and Queen of Heaven on twin thrones; Edward Maitland's God-vision as Mother and Father, Substance and Force. The pattern is durable. [¶132–133]
- **The mother is the first carrier of the anima.** The infant *assimilates* the real mother to the pre-existing archetype of the divine syzygy. With increasing consciousness, the real mother is perceived and the archetype detaches — often to the child's disappointment. [¶137]
- The patient who drew his mother first as "a divine hermaphrodite" and then as a "woeful figure of an ordinary old woman with bloody mutilations" was depicting this detachment, not a literal castration. His "fear of castration" was fear of a life that no longer had mythological meaning. [¶138–139]
- "Actually, nobody can stand the total loss of the archetype." Modern *discontent in our culture* is partly the experience of trying to live without the parental imago at all. [¶141]
- Mature anima-projection moves from mother to lover. Goethe's Frau von Stein resurfacing as Mignon and Gretchen is the canonical case. Boundless fascination, overvaluation, infatuation, *and* its opposite (misogyny) are both anima dynamics — not facts about the woman. [¶141]
- "The male-female syzygy is only one among the possible pairs of opposites, albeit the most important one in practice and the commonest." Kundalini yoga and alchemy contain many non-sexed pairs of opposites. [¶142]
- "An archetype in its quiescent, unprojected state has no exactly determinable form but is in itself an indefinite structure which can assume definite forms only in projection." [¶142]
- **Practical anima-psychology in men.** When the anima is "strongly constellated, she softens the man's character and makes him touchy, irritable, moody, jealous, vain, and unadjusted." He is in *discontent* and spreads it around him. [¶144]
- **Therapeutic threshold ~age 35.** Before mid-life, a man can lose the anima without injury — he needs to be a man. After mid-life, "permanent loss of the anima means a diminution of vitality, of flexibility, and of human kindness." The failure modes: "premature rigidity, crustiness, stereotypy, fanatical one-sidedness, obstinacy, pedantry" or alternatively "resignation, weariness, sloppiness, irresponsibility, and finally a childish *ramollissement* with a tendency to alcohol." [¶146–147]

### Part II: Psychological Aspects of the Mother Archetype

#### §1: On the Concept of the Archetype

- The Great Mother concept comes from comparative religion and rarely appears in clinical practice directly. The wider *mother archetype* is the proper subject for psychology. [¶148]
- The two-hundred-year nominalist consensus (ideas are only names, only what comes from outside is real) is showing cracks. Kant's categories destroyed naive metaphysics but also paved the way for a return of the Platonic Idea as *a priori cognitive structure*. [¶149–150]
- The newborn psyche is not a *tabula rasa*. It is a "tremendously complicated, sharply defined individual entity" with inherited dispositions, just as it has inherited instincts and inherited disease predispositions. [¶151]
- Jung's *contribution* to the archetype concept (already named by Plato, Bastian's "elementary thoughts," Hubert and Mauss's "categories of the imagination"): archetypes can rearise *spontaneously*, at any time, without tradition or migration carrying them. [¶153]
- **The crystal analogy.** "The archetype in itself is empty and purely formal, nothing but a *facultas praeformandi*." Like a crystal's axial system, it determines the *structure* of representation but not the concrete representation. Specific manifestations vary endlessly. [¶155]

#### §2: The Mother Archetype

- The archetype manifests in vast variety: personal mother, grandmother, stepmother, the Mother of God, Sophia, Demeter, Cybele. Also *figurative* mothers: the Church, the university, country, city, sea, woods, the underworld, the moon. Also *fertility symbols*: cornucopia, ploughed field, rock, cave, spring, well, baptismal font, rose, lotus. Also *vessel forms*: ovens, cooking pots, *uterus*, *yoni*. Also helpful animals: cow, hare. [¶156]
- **Negative symbols** of the same archetype: witch, dragon, serpent, grave, sarcophagus, deep water, death, Empusa, Lilith. [¶157]
- The full attribute set: "maternal solicitude and sympathy; the magic authority of the female; the wisdom and spiritual exaltation that transcend reason... On the negative side, anything secret, hidden, dark; the abyss, the world of the dead, anything that devours, seduces, and poisons." Jung calls this "the loving and the terrible mother." [¶158]
- Familiar dual-mother examples: the Virgin Mary as both Christ's mother *and* his cross (medieval allegory); the Indian *Kali* as paradoxical loving-terrible mother; the three *gunas* of Sankhya philosophy (sattva, rajas, tamas — goodness, passion, darkness) as cherishing, orgiastic, and Stygian aspects of the same mother. [¶158]
- **The therapeutic distinction that matters.** Disturbances from the mother divide into (1) traits the mother actually has and (2) traits the child *projects* onto her from the archetype. Personalistic psychology never got beyond (1). Jung's claim: most of the trauma is actually (2). [¶159]
- "Archetypes are among the inalienable assets of every psyche... they are among the highest values of the human psyche." The task is "not to deny the archetype, but to dissolve the projections, in order to restore their contents to the individual." [¶160]

#### §3: The Mother-Complex

- The mother-complex always involves the real mother playing some role. The child's *instincts get disturbed*, which *constellates archetypes*, which then produce frightening fantasy-content between the child and mother. [¶161]
- **In the son**, the mother-complex is "never pure" because it is always entangled with the *anima* (the contrasexual archetype). The son's masculinity and the mother's femininity continually cross. [¶162]
- Negative manifestations in the son: homosexuality (heterosexuality bound unconsciously to mother), *Don Juanism* (unconsciously seeking mother in every woman), impotence. Cybele-Attis pattern: self-castration, madness, early death. [¶162]
- Positive aspects of the same complex in the son: highly differentiated *Eros*, capacity for tender male friendship, aesthetic sense, teaching gift, feeling for history, religious receptivity. [¶164]
- Positive aspect of Don Juanism: "bold and resolute manliness; ambitious striving... opposition to all stupidity, narrow-mindedness, injustice, and laziness... finally, a revolutionary spirit which strives to put a new face upon the world." [¶165]
- **In the daughter**, the mother-complex is "clear and uncomplicated" (no sex-difference confound): either *hypertrophy* of feminine instinct or *atrophy*. [¶163]

##### Mother-complex in the daughter — four variants (¶167–171)

- **(a) Hypertrophy of the maternal element.** "The woman whose only goal is childbirth." Husband is "the instrument of procreation"; her own personality is suppressed. Unconscious *Eros* always expresses as *will to power*. She "annihilates not only her own personality but the personal lives of her children." Baubo rather than Demeter is the apt symbol. [¶167]
- **(b) Overdeveloped Eros.** Maternal instinct wiped out; replaced by an *unconscious incestuous relationship with the father*. Jealousy of mother becomes the leitmotif. Wrecks others' marriages "for their own sake" — interest evaporates once the goal is attained. Hooks for male anima projections. [¶168]
- **(c) Identity with the mother.** Complete projection of personality onto mother; daughter is a "shadow-existence... visibly sucked dry." Paradoxically high market value in marriage: emptiness means the man can "impute anything he fancies." The Persephone-Pluto-Demeter dynamic. [¶169]
- **(d) Resistance to the mother.** "Anything, so long as it is not like Mother!" Knows what she doesn't want; clueless about what to choose. Marriages either escape mother or recapitulate her. Resistance to mother-as-uterus manifests as menstrual disorder, miscarriage, abhorrence of pregnancy. Resistance to mother-as-materia manifests as clumsiness, bad taste. Can also spawn *spontaneous intellectual development* aimed at out-arguing the mother. [¶170–171]

#### §4: Positive Aspects of the Mother-Complex

- **The Mother (positive of hypertrophy).** "The mother-love which is one of the most moving and unforgettable memories... the love that means homecoming, shelter, and the long silence from which everything begins and in which everything ends." A child must be relieved of the weight of identifying real mother with the archetype, for both their sakes. [¶172]
- "A view of the world or a social order that cuts him off from the primordial images of life not only is no culture at all but, in increasing degree, is a prison or a stable." Cut off, the archetype-energy doesn't disappear — it floods back unconsciously and corrupts intellect. [¶174]
- **The Overdeveloped Eros (positive).** The wrecker of comfortable marriages serves a function: she disturbs the sleep of men trapped in maternal solicitude. "She is the disturber, the worker of change, herself caught in the disturbance." Quote from Goethe she embodies: "Part of that power which would / Ever work evil but engenders good." [¶176–181]
- "There is no consciousness without discrimination of opposites. This is the paternal principle, the *Logos*... Conflict engenders fire... emotion is the chief source of consciousness." [¶178–179]
- **The "Nothing-But" Daughter (positive).** The empty woman becomes a vessel for male anima projection. May carry her husband's career on her back through projection of his own undeveloped gift. *Cherchez la femme*. [¶182]
- "Emptiness is a great feminine secret. It is something absolutely alien to man; the chasm, the unplumbed depths, the *yin*." A man's encounter with this either makes him or breaks him. [¶183]
- **The Negative Mother-Complex (positive).** The daughter who fought hard against the mother often makes the best marriage in the second half of life — provided she finally faces forward. Brings "objectivity and coolness of judgment"; becomes "friend, sister, and competent adviser" to a husband. Often plays an unseen but pivotal role as guide to a man whose Eros wanders in dark Hecate territory. [¶184–186]

#### §5: Conclusion

- The archetype-image splits as it recedes from consciousness. The grandmother is "*greater* than the mother" — the *Great Mother* proper. As distance from consciousness grows, opposites in the image split into a good fairy and a wicked fairy. [¶188–189]
- **The West-East split.** Western (Christian) feeling-function forced a moral splitting of the divine into good God and devil; Yahweh's original moral ambivalence was resolved. Eastern intellectual-intuitive attitude preserved paradoxical morality intact — Kali still holds both poles. The Madonna lost her shadow; it migrated to "the devil's grandmother" in folklore. [¶189]
- **Identification with the archetype is catastrophic.** "One ought never to identify with an archetype, for, as psychopathology and certain contemporary events show, the consequences are terrifying." (Written 1938.) [¶189]
- "The unity of consciousness or of the so-called personality is not a reality at all but a *desideratum*. The psyche is a boiling cauldron of contradictory impulses, inhibitions, and affects." [¶190]
- Mother-image type in a man tends toward *Urania* (heavenly); in a woman toward *chthonic* (earth). Their counterparts in the syzygy mirror this: the man identifies with the son-lover (puer aeternus, *filius sapientiae*) of Sophia; the chthonic mother's male counterpart is ithyphallic Hermes (Bes, lingam). [¶192–193]
- **The 1950 Assumption dogma.** Jung reads it as compensatory: at the moment when materialism and the hydrogen bomb threaten total destruction, the Mother is "enthroned in heaven" — a "counterstroke to the materialistic doctrinairism that provoked the chthonic powers into revolt." Symbol of the union of matter and spirit. [¶195–197]
- "The alchemist saw the union of opposites under the symbol of the tree... the tree that is also man." A symbol that "springs from the union of opposites and, by its eternal presence, also makes that union possible." [¶198]

### Part III: Concerning Rebirth

#### §1: Forms of Rebirth

- Five distinct meanings sit under the word "rebirth." Most arguments are about which one is meant. [¶199]
- **Metempsychosis** — transmigration of souls across bodies. Continuity of *karma* but not necessarily of personality. The Buddha refused to answer whether personality continues. [¶200]
- **Reincarnation** — implies continuity of personality and (in principle) memory across human lives. [¶201]
- **Resurrection** — re-establishment of existence after death, often with transformation. Carnal (Christian bodily resurrection) or subtle (the *corpus glorificationis*, the incorruptible body). [¶202]
- **Renovatio** — renewal *within* a single life. Either non-essential (functions are healed, person is unchanged) or essential (total transformation: mortal becomes immortal, corporeal becomes spiritual, human becomes divine). The transfiguration and ascension are essential-renovatio prototypes. [¶203–204]
- **Participation in the process of transformation** — indirect rebirth, achieved by witnessing or taking part in a transformation rite. The Mass, the Eleusinian mysteries, pagan initiations. [¶205]

#### §2: The Psychology of Rebirth

- Rebirth is not sense-perceptible; it is "purely psychic reality, transmitted indirectly through personal statements." That the concept exists, and people speak about it, means the corresponding *psychic experiences* exist. [¶206]
- Rebirth is a *primordial affirmation* (i.e., archetypal) found across cultures. [¶207]
- The phenomenology splits into two main groups: **experience of the transcendence of life** (something is transformed outside you) and **subjective transformation** (you are transformed). [¶207]

##### Experience of the transcendence of life

- **Ritual-induced.** The initiate witnesses or enacts the death-and-rebirth of a god (Osiris dismembered and rising in the green wheat, Christ at the Mass). The transformation is *outside* the initiate but he is "consecrated" by presence. [¶208]
- The Mass is the canonical Western example. Even congregants chatting near the exit participate by being there. "A moment of eternity in time." [¶209]
- **Spontaneous / ecstatic / visionary.** Same content, no ritual scaffolding. Nietzsche's "Noontide Vision" is the canonical example. Many beautiful dreams fall here — impressive but non-transformative. These "aesthetic" experiences are not the same as the structural-change kind. [¶210–211]

##### Subjective transformation — seven sub-types

- **(a) Diminution of personality** (*abaissement du niveau mental*, Janet's term, equivalent to primitive "loss of soul"). Slackening of conscious tonus. Felt as listlessness, depression, no energy. Can extend to hysterical loss of function, dissociation. Triggered by fatigue, illness, shock, violent emotion. [¶213–214]
- **(b) Enlargement of personality.** Real enlargement comes from *inner amplitude*, not from "stuffing in as much as possible from outside." The bigger the man grows, the more the *task* can grow him. Paul on the road to Damascus is the canonical case — the apparition came not from the historical Jesus but from Paul's own unconscious. [¶215–217]
- "When a summit of life is reached... *One becomes Two*, and the greater figure, which one always was but which remained invisible, appears to the lesser personality with the force of a revelation." [¶217]
- **(c) Change of internal structure** = *possession*. "Identity of the ego-personality with a complex." The most common form: **identification with the persona** — the professor with his textbook, the tenor with his voice. "The persona is that which in reality one is not, but which oneself as well as others think one is." The persona is rewarded in cash. [¶220–221]
- Other possessions: by the *inferior function* (= the shadow); by the *anima* (man becomes touchy, irritable, moody, jealous, vain, "bitchy, double-faced, and mystical"); by the *animus* (woman becomes obstinate, harping on principles, "dogmatic, world-reforming, theoretic, word-mongering, argumentative, and domineering"). [¶222–223]
- Both anima and animus, when *turned toward the world*, are corrosive. When *turned away, introverted*, they become bridges to the unconscious. The difference between possession and integration. [¶223]
- Rarer: identification with an "ancestral soul" — a definite forebear takes over. Primitive ancestor-naming rites institutionalised this. [¶224]
- **(d) Identification with a group.** Genuinely different from individual transformation. A group experience happens at a *lower level of consciousness* than an individual experience would. "The psychology of a large crowd inevitably sinks to the level of mob psychology." [¶225]
- Group transformation requires continual mass-intoxication to persist. The individual reverts the moment he leaves the crowd. The group-feeling is a "regressive identification with lower and more primitive states" — exhilarating but cheap. [¶226]
- Ritual *partially counteracts* the regression by giving the group a centre. Without a ritual centre, the mass becomes a "breeding-ground of psychic epidemics — the events in Germany being a classic example." (1939.) [¶227]
- The communist state is the logical endpoint: "an absolute monarchy in which there are no subjects, but only serfs." [¶228]
- **(e) Identification with a cult-hero.** The initiate becomes the god in the ritual (Apuleius crowned as Helios). The Pharaoh "had an Osiris"; later all nobles did; Christianity democratised this further: every believer carries an inner Christ. Many exemplars of the totem animal are eaten, "yet it is only the One who is being eaten." [¶229–230]
- **(f) Magical procedures.** The rite is *used as a technique* to force transformation — pulled through a hole in the wall, given a new name, pulled through a leathern cow, baptismal ablution. The renewal is engineered, not undergone. [¶231]
- **(g) Technical transformation.** Yoga (East) and the *exercitia spiritualia* (West) — disciplined techniques designed to *induce* the transformation that earlier happened spontaneously. The fairy-tale of the old man in the cave drawing the mandala captures the inversion: disciples copy the *result* rather than passing through the *process*. [¶232–233]
- **(h) Natural transformation = individuation.** The transformations Nature herself performs on us, "whether we like it or not." The aim: meeting the *inner friend* — that "larger and greater personality maturing within us." The Dioscuri pair (one mortal, one immortal). "We cannot get accustomed to the idea that we are not absolute master in our own house." [¶234–235]
- "You need not be insane to hear his voice." The conversation with the inner other was technical in Ignatius Loyola (who allowed the meditation but not the *response*) and is dismissed today as "merely associating." [¶236]
- The inner other "is just as one-sided in one way as the ego is in another." A genuine *colloquy* requires the ego to grant the other its rightful personality. [¶237]
- The alchemists projected the inner friend outward as the *Stone*: "Protect me and I will protect thee." Hermes / Mercurius was their mystagogue. The same friend appears as Christ, Khidr, the guru. [¶238]

#### §3: A Typical Set of Symbols — the Eighteenth Sura

- **The Cave.** The Seven Sleepers stay in the middle of the cave, sun passing on either side. The cave is "the place of rebirth, that secret cavity in which one is shut up in order to be incubated and renewed." The "middle" position is where the jewel reposes. [¶240]
- "Anyone who gets into that cave — the cave that everyone has in himself, the darkness that lies behind consciousness — will find himself involved in an unconscious process of transformation." [¶241]
- The sleepers sleep 309 years. The sacred number indicates gods, transformed during sleep, enjoying eternal youth. Hearing the legend integrates parallel unconscious processes in the hearer. [¶242–243]
- **Moses and Khidr.** Moses (the seeker) travels with his "servant" Joshua (his shadow). At "the place where the two seas meet" they forget the fish (their nourishment, the *animal ancestor and creator of life*). The fish leaps back to the sea — Moses has unconsciously found the source of life and lost it. [¶243–245]
- At that spot Khidr appears. The fish's disappearance and Khidr's appearance are linked; Khidr may *be* the fish in transformed form. He is "the Verdant One," born in a cave, long-lived, dismembered at the end of time, the Second Adam, the Paraclete. [¶245–247]
- Khidr is **a symbol of the self.** Moses accepts him as a higher consciousness and looks up for instruction. Khidr's three incomprehensible acts (sinking the boat, killing the youth, raising the wall) show how ego-consciousness must surrender to the self's superior guidance. [¶247]
- **The self emerges from the most improbable source.** It is "the most insignificant of things" *and* "the most significant of all" — the "round" wholeness consciousness lacks. Jung calls it the **Self**: a psychic totality and centre that includes the ego but does not coincide with it. "A larger circle encloses a smaller one." [¶248]
- The intuition of *immortality* during transformation derives from the unconscious's "non-spatial and non-temporal" character. Deification rites are projections of this feeling. [¶249]
- **The Dhulqarnein passage.** Alexander the Great as Khidr's friend, the Dioscuri pair. Moses tells the story as happening to the *other* — because identifying ego-consciousness with the self produces "inflation which threatens consciousness with dissolution." [¶253–254]
- The Faust / Mephistopheles and Nietzsche / Zarathustra pairings are warnings of inflation gone wrong. "The fact that the unimpressive beginning of the transformation in Faust is a dog, and not an edible fish, and that the transformed figure is the devil and not a wise friend... might offer a key to our understanding of the highly enigmatic Germanic soul." [¶254]
- **Gog and Magog.** The rampart must be built against the formless hostile masses — the *collective forces* that threaten the individuated self. "Individuation is an *opus contra naturam*, which creates a *horror vacui* in the collective layer and is only too likely to collapse under the impact of the collective forces of the psyche." [¶256]
- The rampart eventually falls — at the moment of subjective death, when individual consciousness sinks back into the unconscious. [¶256]

### Part IV: The Psychology of the Child Archetype + The Psychological Aspects of the Kore

#### Child Archetype — Introduction

- Primitive man does *not invent* myths; he *experiences* them. "Myths are original revelations of the preconscious psyche, involuntary statements about unconscious psychic happenings, and anything but allegories of physical processes." [¶261]
- A tribe loses its mythology the way a man loses his soul. "Religion is a vital link with psychic processes independent of and beyond consciousness." [¶261]
- Fantasies split into (1) *personal* — explicable by individual anamnesis — and (2) *impersonal* — irreducible to personal experience, parallel to mythological types. The second class forces the hypothesis of a *collective unconscious*. [¶262]
- The child archetype is one of the most variable: Christ-child, dwarf, elf, Goethe's transformed Faust, the alchemists' *anthropárion* (the little metal man in the mine-shaft, Mercurius reborn as *filius sapientiae* or *in-fans noster*). [¶268]
- In dreams the child appears as the dreamer's child, an exotic child, the king's son, the witch's child. Also non-anthropomorphic: jewel, pearl, flower, chalice, golden egg, golden ball, quaternity — all variants of "the treasure hard to attain." [¶270]

#### Child Archetype — Function

- **Archetype as link with the past.** The child motif "represents the preconscious, childhood aspect of the collective psyche." A man cuts himself off from his roots by sundering himself from his original character "in the interests of some arbitrary persona more in keeping with his ambitions" — and is then vulnerable to violent reconfrontation with the primary truth. [¶273–274]
- "Religious observances serve the purpose of bringing the image of childhood, and everything connected with it, again and again before the eyes of the conscious mind so that the link with the original condition may not be broken." [¶275]
- **Compensation.** The conscious mind concentrates on few contents; this *one-sidedness* needs correction. The child motif keeps the unrealised, the natural, the instinctive in view. "For ages man has dreamed of flying, and all we have got for it is saturation bombing!" [¶276]
- The split-off childhood doesn't disappear — it possesses the personality from below. Repression of the childhood-state produces a falsified adult. [¶277]
- **Futurity.** "The child is potential future." It anticipates the figure produced by the synthesis of conscious and unconscious — i.e., it is a symbol of the *Self*. Wholeness symbols (roundness, circle, sphere, quaternity) appear at the *beginning* of individuation, supporting the *entelechy* reading (something already-existent being unfolded). [¶278]
- **Unity vs plurality.** Many homunculi = dissociation (schizophrenia). One = an as-yet-unconscious-but-provisional synthesis. [¶279–280]

#### Child Archetype — Phenomenology

- **Child God vs Child Hero.** Both share miraculous birth and early-childhood persecution. The god is wholly supernatural (un-integrated collective unconscious personified); the hero is human raised to the supernatural — a synthesis of unconscious and human consciousness, the *anticipation of individuation*. [¶281–282]
- **Abandonment.** The child must detach from its origins to evolve. Abandonment isn't a symptom — it's a *necessary condition*. The unconscious produces the *irrational third* (the child) from a conflict-situation the conscious mind cannot resolve. [¶285–287]
- "The Christ Child is a religious necessity only so long as the majority of men are incapable of giving psychological reality to the saying 'Except ye become as little children.'" Mythological figures persist precisely because the corresponding psychological development hasn't yet happened. [¶287]
- **Invincibility.** "Smaller than small yet bigger than big." The child is helpless *and* divine, and the paradox is the essence. The hero fails on something trivial — Baldur on mistletoe, Maui on the laughter of a bird, Siegfried on his one vulnerable spot, Heracles on his wife's gift. [¶283, ¶289]
- The Self is "smaller than small" as an individual phenomenon and "bigger than big" as cosmic. "Only because of this psychic *otherness* is consciousness possible at all. Identity does not make consciousness possible; only separation, detachment, and agonising confrontation through opposition produce consciousness and insight." [¶289]
- **Hermaphroditism.** Cosmogonic gods are typically bisexual. Not just a primitive non-differentiation but a *uniting symbol* that mediates between consciousness and unconscious. The hieros gamos: alchemical *coniugium solis et lunae*, Gospel of the Egyptians' "when the two shall be one." [¶292–295]
- "Children are educated by what the grownup *is* and not by what he *says*. The popular faith in words is a veritable disease of the mind." [¶293]
- **Beginning and End.** The child is "*renatus in novam infantiam*" — symbolising both the pre-conscious (earliest childhood) *and* the post-conscious (anticipation of life after death). [¶299]
- "The 'eternal child' in man is an indescribable experience, an incongruity, a handicap, and a divine prerogative; an imponderable that determines the ultimate worth or worthlessness of a personality." [¶300]

#### Child Archetype — Therapeutic Sequence

The maturation process moves through three identifications:

- **First identification: personal infantilism.** "Abandoned" or "misunderstood" child, overweening pretensions. [¶303–304]
- **Second identification: the hero.** Inflation in two forms: *conscious megalomania* ("I am extraordinary") or *negative inflation* ("the heroic sufferer"). Both are present simultaneously — conscious megalomania balances unconscious inferiority and vice versa. "You never get one without the other." [¶304]
- **Cleared of the hero-identification: synthesis.** Conscious and unconscious are now separable. "Shifting of the centre of personality from the ego to the self." [¶304]

#### The Kore — Psychological Aspects

- The Kore in a *man* belongs to the anima type; in a *woman* she is part of the supraordinate personality (the *Self*) as the maiden complement to the mother. The Demeter-Kore myth has practically no male role except seducer or conqueror — it is "too feminine to have been merely the result of an anima-projection." [¶310, ¶383]
- Practical manifestations: unknown young girl, Gretchen, the dancer (corybant, maenad, nymph), the nixie. Also "slithers down to the animal kingdom": cat, snake, bear, crocodile. [¶311]
- The maiden is exposed to "blood, sacrifice, torture, obscenity" carried out *by an Earth Mother* — the dark side of the supraordinate feminine. [¶311]
- The Earth Mother is "dark deepening to black, or red," with primitive or animal expression, like the neolithic Venus of Brassempouy or Willendorf. [¶312]
- The **Self** is supraordinate; the ego is part of it. The Self "is felt empirically not as subject but as object," coming to consciousness only "indirectly, by way of projection." It is symbolised by human pairs (father-son, mother-daughter, king-queen), theriomorphs (dragon, snake, lion, bear, butterfly, beetle), plants (lotus, rose), geometric forms (circle, sphere, square, quaternity). [¶315]
- "Every mother contains her daughter in herself, and every daughter her mother. Every woman extends backwards into her mother and forwards into her daughter." This is the experiential basis of feminine immortality-intuition. The Demeter cult performed this for ancient Greek women. "Our culture no longer knows the kind of wholesome experience afforded by Eleusinian emotions." [¶316–317]
- The anima in a man is "bipolar and can appear positive one moment and negative the next; now young, now old; now mother, now maiden; now a good fairy, now a witch; now a saint, now a whore." [¶356]
- Age-mapping of the man's anima-projection: "An infantile man generally has a maternal anima; an adult man, the figure of a younger woman. The senile man finds compensation in a very young girl, or even a child." [¶357]
- "The maidens are always doomed to die, because their exclusive domination of the feminine psyche hinders the individuation process." A woman content to be a *femme à homme* "has no feminine individuality; she is empty and merely glitters — a welcome vessel for masculine projections." [¶355]

### Part V: The Phenomenology of the Spirit in Fairytales + The Psychology of the Trickster-Figure

#### Concerning the Word "Spirit"

- "Spirit" carries at least a dozen meanings: principle opposed to matter; supernatural / anti-natural; *ligamentum animae et corporis* (the alchemists); rational intellect; sprightliness; attitude (the "spirit of Pestalozzi"); the time-spirit; the objective spirit (cultural patrimony); the soul of the departed. [¶385–387]
- The *Geist* / *spiritus* etymology: linked to *Gischt* (foam), *Gäscht* (yeast), ghost, *aghast*; to πνεūμα (air in motion), *animus / anima* (related to *anemos*, wind). Spirit is always "active, winged, swift-moving," the dynamic principle, the classic antithesis of matter's stasis. [¶387, ¶389]
- Three hallmarks of spirit: (1) spontaneous movement and activity; (2) spontaneous capacity to produce images independently of sense perception; (3) autonomous, sovereign manipulation of these images. [¶393]
- "He himself did not create the spirit; rather the spirit makes him creative... The primordial phenomenon of the spirit takes possession of him, and, while appearing to be the willing object of human intentions, it binds his freedom with a thousand chains and becomes an obsessive *idée-force*." [¶393]
- Modern man's danger: spirit threatens "inflation" when not balanced by relation to nature. Untethered, it leads to "unbridled materialism" or "the extinction of the autonomous personality, which is in any case the ideal of the totalitarian mass state." [¶393]

#### Self-Representation of the Spirit in Dreams

- The spirit appears most often as the **Wise Old Man** — magician, doctor, priest, teacher, professor, grandfather. In men a positive father-complex produces credulity toward authority and dogma; in women, "the liveliest spiritual aspirations." [¶396]
- "The wise old man... always appears in a situation where insight, understanding, good advice, determination, planning, etc., are needed but cannot be mustered on one's own resources. The archetype compensates this state of spiritual deficiency." [¶398]
- The figure may also appear as a youth — *filius regius* (positive, the higher self) or infantile shadow (negative). "Graybeard and boy belong together. The pair of them play a considerable role in alchemy as symbols of Mercurius." [¶396]
- Moral status is unreliable. "Sometimes the *probate spiritus* recommended by John cannot be anything other than a cautious and patient waiting to see how things will finally turn out." [¶397]

#### The Spirit in Fairytales

- The old man appears when the hero is hopeless or trapped. He represents "purposeful reflection and concentration of moral and physical forces that comes about spontaneously in the psychic space outside consciousness." [¶402]
- He asks *who, why, whence, whither* — questions that *force self-reflection* and so mobilise the moral forces. He often gives a *magical talisman* (the surplus of unified-personality power). [¶404]
- His advice often arrives as: "Lie down: morning is cleverer than evening." The unconscious solves while consciousness sleeps. [¶405]
- He has a **dark side** too. The Balkan one-eyed old man (the *Vili* gouged his eye out) parallels Wotan sacrificing his eye at Mimir's spring and Osiris losing his eye to Set. [¶413]
- "The very word *pharmakon* means 'poison' as well as 'antidote.'" The old man is *ad utrumque peritus* — skilled in both, like Hermes. [¶414]
- Merlin is the most instructive case of the ambiguous Wise Old Man. He is "the wicked magician who, from sheer egoism, does evil for evil's sake" in some forms and pure goodness in others. [¶415]
- The witch / dark-mother counterpart often appears in the same story. The "old man" and "old woman" together form the negative parental imagos of the unconscious. [¶427]

#### Theriomorphic Spirit Symbolism — the Three-Legged and Four-Legged Horse

- A long worked example from the German *swineherd-and-princess* fairytale. The young hero must rescue a princess held by a wicked hunter (a Wotan-figure) who rides a three-legged white horse. To break the spell he must steal a four-legged horse from a witch. [¶422–425]
- **The three / four axiom (alchemy's "axiom of Maria"):** *Out of the third comes the one as the fourth.* The differentiated psyche has three conscious functions plus one inferior function "still rooted in" the unconscious. The fourth, "always returning unbidden, like an *imp* or *deus ex machina*," is the wound through which the unconscious enters. [¶425, ¶430–432]
- The conflict in the tale is between two triads — the upper triad (the three nails crucifying the raven) and the lower triad (the wicked hunter's three-legged horse). Both point in opposite directions; both are *mutilated quaternities*. [¶425–426, ¶430]
- Resolution: the hero captures the four-legged horse (assimilates the inferior function) and the conflict dissolves. The hunter "melts into thin air." [¶434]
- "The hero lies dormant in the hunter from the very beginning." The villain and hero are the same archetype split in two — once integrated, the figure of the hunter is reabsorbed. [¶434]

#### Conclusion

- "Spirit was originally a spirit in human or animal form, a *daimonion* that came upon man from without." Modernity has *introjected* the daimon, claiming all spirit-phenomena are "only" human spirit. [¶454]
- This integration is dangerous: "the superhuman spiritual agencies that were formerly tied up in nature are introjected into human nature, thus endowing it with a power which extends the bounds of the personality *ad infinitum*, in the most perilous way." [¶454]
- "Has rational reduction led to the beneficial control of matter and spirit?... Man has achieved a wealth of useful gadgets, but, to offset that, he has torn open the abyss." [¶454]
- "Man's worst sin is unconsciousness." [¶455]

#### On the Psychology of the Trickster-Figure

- The trickster appears in: Mercurius (the alchemist's shape-shifting half-divine prankster), Tom Thumb, Hanswurst, medieval *carnival*, the medieval Fools' Pope (*festum stultorum*), the donkey festival (*festum asinorum*), and Paul Radin's Winnebago trickster cycle. [¶456, ¶458–461]
- **The medieval Fools' Holiday** as ecclesiastical trickster-residue. In Beauvais the whole congregation brayed "Y-a" like a donkey at each part of the Mass; the priest brayed three times at the close instead of *Ite missa est*. "The more ridiculous this rite seemed, the greater the enthusiasm with which it was celebrated." [¶461–463]
- Parapsychological parallels: **poltergeists** (around pre-adolescents) — malicious tricks, low-level intelligence, fatuous "communications," shape-changing, the motif of subjective suffering. The shaman's "approximation to the saviour" follows the same pattern: the wounded wounder is the agent of healing. [¶457]
- **The trickster as collective shadow.** "I have, I think, found a suitable designation for this character-component when I called it the shadow. On the civilized level, it is regarded as a personal 'gaffe,' 'slip,' '*faux pas*.'" The personal shadow descends from a numinous collective figure. [¶469]
- The trickster's bodily portrait (Winnebago): "His body is not a unity, and his two hands fight each other. He takes his anus off and entrusts it with a special task. Even his sex is optional despite its phallic qualities... From his penis he makes all kinds of useful plants." [¶472]
- "He is a forerunner of the saviour, and, like him, God, man, and animal at once... His chief and most alarming characteristic is his unconsciousness." [¶472]
- The trickster myth is sustained because it *therapeutically anamneses* the prior state of consciousness. Civilization repeatedly forgets its origins; the myth keeps the door open. [¶474, ¶480]
- "The civilized man has forgotten the trickster. He remembers him only figuratively when, irritated by his own ineptitude, he speaks of fate playing tricks on him... As soon as people get together in masses and submerge the individual, the shadow is mobilized, and, as history shows, may even be personified and incarnated." [¶478]
- "Nothing is ever lost, not even the blood pact with the devil. Outwardly it is forgotten, but inwardly not at all." [¶481]
- **The way of individuation begins with the shadow.** "A minatory and ridiculous figure, he stands at the very beginning of the way of individuation, posing the deceptively easy riddle of the Sphinx." Behind the shadow stands the *anima*; behind her, the *wise old man*. [¶485–486]
- "The recognition and unavoidable integration of the shadow create such a harrowing situation that nobody but a saviour can undo the tangled web of fate." [¶487]

### Part VI: Conscious, Unconscious, and Individuation + A Study in the Process of Individuation + Concerning Mandala Symbolism

#### Conscious, Unconscious, and Individuation — the theoretical essay

- "I use the term *individuation* to denote the process by which a person becomes a psychological 'in-dividual,' that is, a separate, indivisible unity or 'whole.'" [¶490]
- "The ego can be no more than the centre of the field of consciousness." The whole psyche includes the illimitable field of unconscious occurrences. [¶491]
- The unconscious is *not* a "second ego." Janet attributed it to weakness of consciousness; Freud to repression; both built theories from neurosis. Psychiatric experience reveals contents "so strange that nobody can understand them, neither the patient himself nor his doctors." This argues for an independent layer. [¶492–494]
- **The autonomy of the unconscious begins where emotions are generated.** "Affects are not 'made' or wilfully produced; they simply happen." The unconscious can take over the ego in violent affect — and "groups, communities, and even whole nations can be seized in this way by psychic epidemics." [¶496–497]
- "The unconscious has a Janus-face: on one side its contents point back to a preconscious, prehistoric world of instinct, while on the other side it potentially anticipates the future." [¶498]
- "Whereas we think in periods of years, the unconscious thinks and lives in terms of millennia." [¶499]
- "*Universal consciousness* is logically identical with unconsciousness." Jung's critique of yogic *samadhi*: the unconscious has swallowed ego-consciousness. The result is "all-embracing, but nebulous." Not recommended "anywhere north of the Tropic of Cancer." [¶520]
- The European answer is *not* to suppress the unconscious or to dissolve into it but **open conflict and open collaboration at once.** "It is the old game of hammer and anvil: between them the patient iron is forged into an indestructible whole, an 'individual.'" [¶522]
- "How the harmonizing of conscious and unconscious data is to be undertaken cannot be indicated in the form of a recipe. It is an irrational life-process which expresses itself in definite symbols." [¶524]
- Jung calls the union of opposites the **transcendent function**. This rounding-out of the personality is the goal of any psychotherapy that claims to be more than symptom-relief. [¶524]

#### A Study in the Process of Individuation — the Miss X case

- The full case study covers a 55-year-old American woman (Miss X) over a decade. 24 paintings document the progression. Picture-by-picture commentary is omitted here; the load-bearing theoretical observations are extracted below.
- **The signature of individuation is mandala-formation.** Symbols of wholeness (circle, square, quaternity, lotus, sphere) appear spontaneously when the individuation process activates. They "express order, balance, and wholeness" and characteristically appear after "chaotic, disordered states marked by conflict and anxiety." [¶645]
- "The constellation of archetypal images and fantasies is not in itself pathological. The pathological element only reveals itself in the way the individual reacts to them." [¶621]
- **Identification with the archetype is the failure mode.** "You do not 'make' an identification, you do not 'identify yourself,' but you experience your identity with the archetype in an unconscious way and so are possessed by it." [¶621]
- "These pictures are intuitive anticipations of future developments... it is worth while lingering over them for a long time, in order, with their help, to integrate so many contents of the unconscious into consciousness that the latter really does reach the stage it sees ahead." [¶617]
- **The civilisational diagnosis.** "The tempo of the development of consciousness through science and technology was too rapid and left the unconscious far behind, thereby forcing it into a defensive position which expresses itself in a universal will to destruction." (1950s.) The political isms "preach every conceivable ideal, but, under this mask, they pursue the goal of lowering the level of our culture by restricting or altogether inhibiting the possibilities of individual development." [¶617]
- "This problem cannot be solved collectively, because the masses are not changed unless the individual changes." [¶618]
- Jung kept the mandala observations *unpublished for thirteen years* (from ~1916 to 1929) "in order to avoid any suggestion" — i.e., to be sure his patients were producing them spontaneously, not in response to his theory. [¶623]
- The Origen quote Jung uses to interpret Picture 12 (the menagerie of animals): *"Seek these sacrifices within thyself... thou hast within thyself flocks of cattle... Marvel not if we say that these are within thee, but understand that thou thyself art even another little world."* The animals are affects; individuation subordinates the many to the One. [¶624–625]

#### Concerning Mandala Symbolism

- *Mandala* = Sanskrit for "circle." Used in religious ritual (Indian *yantra*, Tibetan ritual circle drawn in coloured chalks). The "best and most significant mandalas are found in the sphere of Tibetan Buddhism." [¶629]
- Structure of the classic Tibetan mandala: three outer circles (often containing the fire of *concupiscentia* and the burial-ground horrors); a garland of lotus leaves; a monastery courtyard with four gates; the four basic colours (red, green, white, yellow = the four directions / four psychic functions); a central magic circle containing Shiva, the One Existent. [¶630]
- "From their splitting arises, in a gigantic explosion of energy, the multiplicity of the world." Creation = the splitting of opposites united in the deity (Shiva-Shakti). The mandala is the depiction of this and its reversal. [¶632–633]
- **Mandalas express "the premonition of a centre of personality, a kind of central point within the psyche, to which everything is related, by which everything is arranged, and which is itself a source of energy."** This centre is not the ego — it is the **Self**. [¶634]
- The self is "on the one hand simple, on the other an extremely composite thing, a 'conglomerate soul.'" It contains consciousness, personal unconscious, and an indefinitely large segment of the collective unconscious. [¶634]
- **Mandalas occur typically in conditions of psychic dissociation or disorientation** — children of divorcing parents (aged 8-11), neurotics confronted with the problem of opposites, schizophrenics whose world has become confused. They are "self-healing on the part of Nature." [¶714]
- The nine formal elements of mandala symbolism: (1) circle / sphere / egg; (2) circle elaborated into flower or wheel; (3) centre with sun / star / cross with four / eight / twelve rays; (4) rotation (swastika); (5) snake coiled round centre (uroboros, Orphic egg); (6) squared circle; (7) castle / city / temenos; (8) eye (pupil and iris); (9) triadic or pentadic figures — these are "disturbed totality pictures." [¶646]
- "Squaring of the circle" is *the archetype of wholeness*. The "quaternity of the One" is the schema for all God-images (Ezekiel, Daniel, Enoch, Horus and his four sons, the four evangelists — three synoptic, one Gnostic). [¶715]
- The 3 / 4 axiom: "ever since the opening of Plato's *Timaeus* ('One, two, three... but where, my dear Socrates, is the fourth?') and right up to the Cabiri scene in Faust, the motif of four as three and one was the ever-recurring preoccupation of alchemy." [¶715]
- Individual mandalas, unlike ritual mandalas, "make use of a well-nigh unlimited wealth of motifs." They often display a division into *light and dark* halves with their typical symbols — Böhme's mandala from *XL Questions concerning the Soule* is the classic example. [¶717]
- "There must be a transconscious disposition in every individual which is able to produce the same or very similar symbols at all times and in all places... Knowledge of the common origin of these unconsciously preformed symbols has been totally lost to us. In order to recover it, we have to read old texts and investigate old cultures." [¶711–712]
- "These images... often represent very bold attempts to see and put together apparently irreconcilable opposites and bridge over apparently hopeless splits. Even the mere attempt in this direction usually has a healing effect, but only when it is done spontaneously. **Nothing can be expected from an artificial repetition or a deliberate imitation of such images.**" [¶718]
