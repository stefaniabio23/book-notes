## Black Edge: Inside Information, Dirty Money, and the Quest to Bring Down the Most Wanted Man on Wall Street

by Sheelah Kolhatkar

*Notes I took while reading this book to further my own learning. If you enjoy these notes, please [buy the book](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/229702/black-edge-by-sheelah-kolhatkar/).*

### Prologue: The Flip

- "Black edge" is Wall Street slang for proprietary, nonpublic, market-moving information. It's the most valuable kind of edge. Trading on it is usually illegal.
- The decade-long government investigation that defines the book began as a wiretap on Raj Rajaratnam (Galleon Group) and ended pointed at Steven Cohen (SAC Capital).
- The Galleon investigation worked because insider trading wasn't a single ring — it was an overlapping network. Each insider trading circle led to two or three more.
- The Akamai trade is the template: trader gets a "guide down" tip from a corporate insider via an intermediary, shorts 875,000 shares, makes $5M in a week. Source kept $2.5M.
- The FBI's path to Cohen ran through Ali Far and C.B. Lee — Galleon and SAC alumni who started Spherix Capital and got flipped in April 2009 as informants.
- Hedge funds began as small boutiques: long the best companies, short the worst, "hedged." By the mid-2000s the name had lost any connection to the strategy and meant "unregulated investment firm doing whatever it wants."
- The standard fee structure is 2-and-20 — 2% of assets, 20% of profits. A $2B fund collects $40M a year before producing a dollar of return.
- In 2006 the lowest-paid manager on the top-25 list made $240M; the top three each made over $1B. Cohen ranked fifth at $900M.
- SAC's distinguishing feature was its consistency: ~30% average annual returns for 20 years, with no clearly articulated strategy. That's the pattern that drew federal attention.
- The mid-2000s arms race forced funds to industrialize edge: lay cable closer to exchanges, hire scientists and shrinks, walk Walmart aisles, study parking-lot satellites. "Once the top cyclists started doping, you either went along with them or you lost."

### Part One

#### Ch 1: Money, Money, Money

- Wall Street draws two archetypes — the prep-school inheritors who arrive entitled, and the scrappy outsiders with something to prove. Cohen is the second.
- Cohen joined Gruntal & Co. in January 1978, fresh from Wharton. Gruntal was an obscure, primarily Jewish brokerage that had survived since 1880 by staying small enough to ignore.
- His first mentor, Ron Aizer, ran an option-arbitrage book exploiting price misalignments between options exchanges and the NYSE. The premise: in theory, calls, puts, and the underlying must be in mathematical lockstep; in practice, communication lags between exchanges created exploitable gaps.
- The arb strategy required almost no risk, no leverage, and most positions closed by end of day. Aizer's principle: "You didn't have to be good. You had to follow the formulas. A trained monkey could do it."
- Cohen made $8,000 on a discretionary bet on his first day. Aizer's reaction: "I knew he was going to be famous within a week. I never saw talent like that."
- The home backdrop: father in the garment district selling $20 dresses to Macy's; mother a piano teacher dominating the family. Cohen learned about money early from his maternal grandparents, who lived off an inherited investment portfolio and represented an "alluring life of freedom."
- Cohen's high school education in capitalism was the poker table — winning $1,000 a night from friends taught him there were difficult ways to make money (stock-boy at $1.85/hour, "excruciating") and easy ways (beating his friends).
- At Wharton he was at the bottom of the social hierarchy. His response was to skip class and stand at the Philadelphia-Baltimore-Washington exchange or watch the Merrill Lynch ticker, training intuition: "I could hear the tick tick tick of the tape... I found I was pretty good at guessing which way those numbers would go."
- Cohen's edge was tape-reading — an intuitive feel for short-term supply and demand. Helen Clarke (Aizer's clerk): "He had an ability to hold on to a position without flexing. He never got out because he was nervous."
- He soon abandoned Aizer's riskless arbitrage strategy and started running unhedged directional positions. By the mid-1980s he was making $5–10M a year personally.
- Reagan-era deregulation, the Milken junk-bond machine, and a wave of leveraged hostile takeovers turned mid-1980s Wall Street into "deal mania." Every rumour was true. Cohen built around takeovers, IPOs, event-driven swings.
- December 1985 RCA trade: Cohen phoned his brother Donald in Miami suggesting RCA "might be restructured" — bought call options on rumour of a takeover. Six days later GE announced the acquisition. Cohen made $20M.
- The SEC subpoena that followed is the first regulatory shot. Cohen's lawyer Otto Obermaier ran the textbook white-collar defense: refuse documents, take the Fifth without saying it on the record, run out the clock.
- The takeaway from the RCA investigation, which "petered out without charges": *Taking the Fifth takes the momentum out of a securities investigation.* That lesson sits behind every later SAC defense.
- Black Monday (19 October 1987, Dow −23%) nearly wiped out Gruntal's group. Cohen's response under chaos defined his style: "From now on, no one is trading but me. You guys are my clerks." He shorted Dow names against bad opens for weeks afterward.
- The chapter ends on the same beat the book will hit again: Cohen survives the storm by keeping cooler than anyone else and using the disorder to compound his edge. His marriage to Patricia doesn't survive — Black Monday year overlaps with the start of acrimonious divorce negotiations.
- Cohen's quote to his traders the day after his divorce settlement, on cutting their payouts to fund the gap: "I just got ripped off by my wife. I'm going to make it all back by cutting your payouts." When one objected, he said: "Fuck you. I'm the boss." This is the establishing shot for everything that follows.

#### Ch 2: What Stevie Wants, Stevie Gets

- SAC launched 1992: $23M of capital, nine employees, Cohen put in ~$10M of his own. The trigger for leaving Gruntal was regulatory: as part of a registered broker-dealer, his group was shut out of IPOs — the new profit pool of the era.
- The hiring filter from day one: athletes, competitive guys, "guys who have the confidence to be out there, to be risk-takers." Cohen's stated goal was a room full of mini-Steve-Cohens.
- Cohen's self-diagnosis as a trader: "5 percent of his trades accounted for most of the money he made." Concentration in the right moments was the whole game; the rest was discipline.
- Closest comparable in style: Michael Steinhardt — high-volume individual investor who demanded better prices than ordinary investors from big brokers in exchange for outsized commissions. Cohen ran the same playbook with bigger volume two decades later.
- The candid summary of Cohen's edge: not math, not macro, not philosophy. "He was just a great trader, one who was so good that a traditional Wall Street career couldn't contain him."
- By year 3, SAC had quadrupled to ~$100M. J.P. Morgan refused to do business with him on suspicion that 100% annual returns had to be cheating.
- Kenny Lissak — Cohen's only real Wall Street relationship-builder — joined and became the operations counterweight. "Steve was the mad scientist and Kenny was the people person." Lissak's contacts at Goldman, Merrill, Lehman opened SAC's access to research and IPO allocations.
- The risk-management lesson came from one trade: Northern Telecom, $38 → $31, Cohen and Lissak averaged down convinced the worst was over, lost ~$2M. The rule that fell out of it: *if a trade goes against you, you set a limit and you sell, no matter what.* Cut the loss, never let ego defend the position.
- 50/50 split on profits, eventually 50% performance fee — vs the industry standard 20%. Investors paid it anyway. "If investors wanted the best — him — they were going to have to pay for it."
- Ari Kiev — Olympic-athletes psychiatrist Cohen hired as in-house trader-coach. Weekly group therapy sessions on overcoming fear of size. "Trading not to lose is not a good strategy. You need to trade to win." Many traders thought Kiev was a fraud or, worse, an internal spy.
- Kiev's dual function: confidence-building for the trader population *and* surveillance for Cohen. Traders avoided opening up because everything got reported back.
- Cohen's marriage to Alex Garcia (June 1992, Plaza Hotel, 300 guests, Lissak the best man) became the inflection point in his personal life. The Cristina Show appearance — eight months in, on a Latin daytime TV show about toxic in-law dynamics — is the chapter's set-piece on how completely Alex reshaped a previously cautious, shy man.
- The Lissak firing (October 1997): Alex accused Lissak of trying to instigate an affair; Cohen fired him cold the same day. Lissak had just spent a year recovering from a near-fatal spinal E. coli infection; Alex had moved into the operational vacuum. The chapter doesn't claim what was true — it shows the pattern: anyone whose share of the upside grew too visibly became expendable.
- Larger theme of the chapter: Cohen's resentment of anyone benefiting from his success — broker commissions, partner shares, NYC taxes, eventually his closest partner. The move to Stamford, CT was tax arbitrage; the firing of Lissak was the same instinct applied to people.

#### Ch 3: Murderers' Row

- By 1998-99 SAC crossed $1B AUM after five years of nearly doubling annually. The drawback Cohen could no longer ignore: his day-trading style only worked at smaller capital, and competitor crowding into his ideas was eroding edge.
- The diagnosis Cohen reached: he needed analyst-grade specialists, not just tape-readers. *"From now on, I want to hire only traders who had a 'fundamental edge.'"* Day-trader gamblers were assigned sectors or fired.
- The new hires — David Ganek (ex-DLJ risk arb), Larry Sapanski (ex-Morgan Stanley energy), others — were the Ivy-League, well-connected breed Cohen had previously avoided. Seated near him, the cluster became *"murderers' row"* — Yankees 1920s reference.
- Richard Grodin (semiconductor specialist since 1992) made the transition. ~$30-40M book, 30%+ returns — *"the highest at the firm on a risk-adjusted basis."* But Grodin's analyst Richard Choo-Beng Lee was the load-bearing asset. *"No one had better connections to Asian technology manufacturers."*
- The Lee information was so valuable that Cohen built an internal surveillance tool — the *"eye in the sky"* — that surfaced every SAC trade order before execution, so Cohen could front-run his own employees. Grodin escaped to the Sigma Capital satellite office in Manhattan to get away.
- The Grodin-Cohen rupture: January 2004 IM exchange. Cohen demanded Lee write up formal reports rather than passing datapoints to Grodin alone. *"Than quit. Rules are the same for everybody."* Grodin resigned next day.
- The hiring machine that replaced them: Sol Kumin (ex-Johns Hopkins lacrosse, "King Midas") as head of business development. Explicit screening criterion was *"personal connections the trader had with people working at public companies that might yield valuable intelligence."* Friends, fraternity brothers, fathers-in-law, wives — *"They could all prove valuable when it came to getting information."*
- Matthew Grossman (Deerfield → Columbia → Tiger Management intern) proposed *"CR Intrinsic"* in late 2004 — a Navy-SEAL research unit modelled on Buffett-style fundamental investing. CR = Cumulative Return. Conditions: PM job if it failed, % of unit P&L, equivalent SAC authority to Cohen himself. Cohen agreed.
- Grossman's office reputation: nickname *"Milhouse"*, $10M/yr before 30, blue Aston Martin (the *Goldfinger* model), unliked but feared. He stayed because Cohen was infatuated; staff fell silent when he walked into rooms.
- Sandy Heller (Steinberg family friend) becomes Cohen's art advisor. The Damien Hirst shark in formaldehyde ($8M), then in 2006 the Picasso *Le Rêve* — agreed price $139M from Steve Wynn, then a record. Wynn celebrating with NYC friends (Ephron, Pileggi, Walters, Boies) elbowed his arm through the canvas the same weekend. Sale paused. *"It's a picture, it's my picture, we'll fix it. Nobody got sick or died."*
- Mathew Martoma (Sirios Capital biotech PM, Stanford MBA) recruited by Kumin in 2006: $400M book, 17% of his own P&L, share of any Cohen P&L from his ideas, $2M signing bonus on a $200K base. The recruitment memo noted his network of doctors *"in the field"* — flagged in his SAC file as the qualifying asset.
- Martoma is the chapter's set-piece. Indian-immigrant family, status-obsessed parenting, doctor wife Rosemary as second co-pilot, 4am-to-late-night work cycle. *"He was a thinker, not a yeller, with all the right credentials."* He pre-existing interest in Alzheimer's from undergraduate volunteering at Duke.
- Bapineuzumab thesis: Elan + Wyeth co-developing the Alzheimer's drug *"bapi"*, improvement on the AN-1792 precursor (withdrawn for brain swelling). Martoma made it his focus.
- The mechanism that connects Martoma to what comes later: Gerson Lehrman Group (GLG) — the *"expert network"* matching investors with employees at public companies at ~$1,000/hr+. SAC's annual subscription was $1.2M. The doctrinal cover: experts were told *not* to share material nonpublic information; in practice the line was *"fuzzy."*
- Martoma's 2006-08-30 GLG query: list of 22 medical experts involved in the bapi trials. *"Are any of these docs in your database?"* One reply set up the rest of the book — *"I am Chair of the Safety Monitoring Committee for this trial. Although I know more than is publicly available, I have a confidentiality agreement and will share only information that is openly available, of course."* The doctor: Sid Gilman, U Michigan neurology endowed chair, age 70s.
- The chapter ends on Gilman's name. Pure setup; the payoff is what the book is building toward — the largest insider-trading case in US history.
- Reading the chapter as portrait of incentive design: SAC's compensation scheme made *information arbitrage the single highest-paid skill at the firm.* Every other choice — surveillance software, sector specialisation, hiring screen for personal connections, GLG subscription, Martoma's 17% personal cut — falls out of that incentive structure.
