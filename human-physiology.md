### Ch 1: What is Physiology?

- *Physiology* studies how living matter performs daily activities, assuming organisms are intricate machines governed by the laws of physics and chemistry. [p.1]
- Most physiological knowledge derives from animal studies (frog, rabbit, cat, dog); applied to humans where the mechanism is conserved across species. [p.1]
- Anatomy is the prerequisite. You cannot study how an organism works without first knowing how it is built. [p.1]
- Hierarchy of the body: *cells* form *tissues* (epithelial, connective, nervous, muscular), which aggregate into *organs*, which combine into *organ systems*. [p.1]
- Three universal features of cells: a bounding plasma membrane, the ability to liberate energy by breaking down large molecules, and possession of a nucleus with DNA at some life stage. [p.1-2]
- *Metabolism* is the sum of cellular chemical transformations. *Catabolism* breaks large molecules into small; *anabolism* synthesises large from small. [p.2]
- *Differentiation* in evolution and embryology splits cells into contractile (muscle), conductive (nerve), and secretory (endocrine, exocrine) lineages from a common origin. [p.2]
- Most tissues mix cell types: blood holds red cells, white cells, and platelets; nervous tissue holds neurons and glia. [p.2]
- The cardiovascular system exists because cells of large multicellular animals cannot extract oxygen and nutrients directly from the external environment. [p.2]
- The heart is a paired pump: right ventricle to lungs for oxygenation, left ventricle to body for delivery. The lymphatic system drains tissue fluid back into the blood. [p.2]
- *Respiration* is the oxidation of sugars and fats to release energy. Oxygen enters via the lungs, carbon dioxide exits in expired air. [p.2]
- Digestive flow: mouth, enzymatic breakdown in the gut, absorption across the intestinal wall, portal vein, liver, tissues. [p.2-3]
- Kidneys regulate the composition of the extracellular fluid by producing urine of variable composition; the bladder buffers the elimination. [p.3]
- Two coordinating systems regulate the body: nerves transmit fast electrical signals to specific targets; hormones travel slowly through the blood for broader regulation. [p.3]
- Organ systems are interdependent. Kidney failure disrupts internal regulation and propagates dysfunction through the rest of the body. [p.3]
- *Homeostasis* is the stabilisation of the internal environment against external and internal disturbance. It is the precondition for normal cell function. [p.3]
- Extracellular potassium illustrates why homeostasis matters: a small excess makes cardiac muscle cells too excitable and produces arrhythmia. [p.3]
- *Balance*: for any body constituent, intake equals use plus excretion at steady state. Body weight stays near-constant despite ~30 kg of food and ~75 L of fluid per month. [p.3]
- *Negative nitrogen balance* in starvation: protein breakdown exceeds intake as muscle is catabolised for glucose. *Positive nitrogen balance* in growth, pregnancy, and early training. [p.3]
- Plasma glucose holds at 4-5 mmol/L between meals via insulin. A postprandial rise drives insulin secretion, which falls as glucose normalises. [p.3]
- *Negative feedback loop* requires three components: a sensor specific to one variable, a comparator with a set point, and an effector that opposes the deviation. [p.3]
- Three limits of negative feedback: it acts only after disturbance, correction is proportional to error and therefore incomplete, and overcorrection causes oscillation. [p.4]
- *Push-pull* control sharpens regulation: insulin lowers glucose while glucagon raises it. Two opposing effectors give tighter control than one. [p.4]
- Negative feedback loops get reset under stress. If baroreceptor-driven blood pressure control did not reset, exercise capacity would be sharply capped. [p.4]
- *Positive feedback* amplifies the initial deviation and is inherently unstable. The textbook analogy is the howl of a microphone held to its own loudspeaker. [p.4]
- Ovulation runs on a positive-feedback switch: sustained high estradiol-17β flips FSH and LH regulation from negative to positive, producing the mid-cycle surge. [p.4]

### Ch 12: The Hormonal Regulation of the Body

The chapter is the textbook's longest single survey and the empirical case study for the regulatory architecture sketched in Ch 1. Where Ch 1 named negative feedback, push-pull, and positive feedback as abstract control patterns, Ch 12 walks the body's nine principal endocrine systems and shows each pattern instantiated in actual tissue. Reading this chapter is essentially reading Ch 1's atoms cashed out across the major hormones, with anatomy and chemistry filled in.

The chapter opens by widening the classical definition of *hormone*. A hormone, in the strict sense, is a blood-borne chemical messenger secreted by a discrete endocrine gland and travelling to a distant target. The modern definition extends to "non-classical" endocrine organs: the heart (atrial natriuretic peptide), the kidney (erythropoietin, 1,25-dihydroxycholecalciferol), the liver (IGF-1, IGF-2, hepcidin), the gastrointestinal tract (cholecystokinin, gastrin, secretin, motilin), platelets, lymphocytes, adipose tissue (leptin), and certain tumour cells. The principal endocrine glands are the hypothalamus, pituitary, thyroid, parathyroid, adrenals, pancreas, ovaries, and testes (Fig 12.1, [p.189]). The chapter's first conceptual move is therefore that the boundary between endocrine and non-endocrine tissue is conventional, not real: any cell that secretes a substance acting at a distance is performing an endocrine function.

*Neurosecretion* is the chapter's bridge to Ch 5. Many hypothalamic neurons synthesise hormones that they secrete into the hypophyseal portal blood; the posterior pituitary's hormones are similarly synthesised by neurons whose axons terminate in the gland. Some peptides (gastrin, cholecystokinin) act as both hormones in the bloodstream and neurotransmitters in the CNS. The nervous and endocrine systems are therefore not parallel architectures but a single continuum operating on different time-scales, and the same molecule can perform either role depending on where it is released. [p.191]

Hormones fall into three chemical categories. **Steroids** are derivatives of cholesterol: the gonadal hormones, adrenal cortical hormones, and the active metabolites of vitamin D. They are hydrophobic, carried in the blood largely bound to specific plasma proteins (testosterone-binding globulin, cortisol-binding globulin, thyroid-hormone-binding globulin), and have long plasma half-lives because protein-bound hormone is cleared slowly. **Peptides** are the largest category, ranging from TRH (3 amino acids) to FSH (~200 amino acids); they travel in free solution and have short half-lives. **Amino acid derivatives** are the catecholamines (from tyrosine) and the thyroid hormones (two iodinated tyrosines linked); catecholamines act like peptides (free solution, short half-life), thyroid hormones act like steroids (protein-bound, long half-life). [p.192]

The chemical category also determines the receptor architecture. Steroids and thyroid hormones diffuse into cells and bind intracellular receptors that alter gene expression directly. Peptides and catecholamines bind plasma-membrane receptors and act either through second messengers (Ca²⁺, cyclic AMP, IP₃) or by activating membrane-bound kinases that themselves alter gene expression. The chapter therefore predicts that lipid-soluble hormones produce slow, sustained effects mediated by new protein synthesis, while water-soluble hormones produce fast, transient effects mediated by modification of existing proteins. [p.192]

Two regulatory patterns recur throughout the chapter. The first is **circadian rhythm**: ACTH (and therefore cortisol), growth hormone, and prolactin all show 24-hour cycles, which means a single plasma measurement of any of these is uninterpretable without time-of-day data. The second is **negative feedback inside hypothalamo-pituitary-target axes**: the target gland's hormone feeds back on both the anterior pituitary and the hypothalamus to inhibit the upstream signal. The TRH→TSH→T₃/T₄ axis is the chapter's textbook example, with T₃/T₄ inhibiting both TSH release and pituitary responsiveness to TRH. The same architecture applies to ACTH/cortisol, the gonadotropins/sex steroids, and (uniquely) prolactin, which is negative-fed-back not by a target gland but by its own action stimulating hypothalamic dopamine. [pp.192, 196] Positive feedback appears only in the female reproductive cycle (estrogens producing the pre-ovulatory FSH/LH surge, see Ch 1 notes) and in parturition (oxytocin released in response to uterine stretching). This matches the Ch 1 prediction: the body reserves positive feedback for one-shot switches, not ongoing regulation.

The chapter's anatomical centrepiece is the **hypothalamo-pituitary system**. The pituitary has two embryologically distinct halves. The *adenohypophysis* (anterior pituitary, derived from buccal ectoderm via Rathke's pouch) has no direct neural input from the hypothalamus and is instead connected by a portal blood system: the *hypophyseal portal vessels* carry hypothalamic releasing and inhibiting hormones from the median eminence down the pituitary stalk to the anterior lobe. The *neurohypophysis* (posterior pituitary, derived from the neural ectoderm of the infundibulum) is a direct nerve-tract extension of the hypothalamus; its hormones are made in supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei and transported down axons to be released at terminals in the posterior lobe. The chapter's anatomy is therefore the answer to a design question: how do you couple a slow chemical signal (the anterior pituitary's secretion) to a fast neural signal (the posterior pituitary's release) without ever letting the chemical signal enter the general circulation in dilute form? Answer: a small local portal system upstream of the slow gland; a direct axonal pathway upstream of the fast one. [pp.193, 197]

The six anterior pituitary hormones are produced by five identifiable cell types (somatotrophs → GH, lactotrophs → prolactin, corticotrophs → ACTH and related peptides, thyrotrophs → TSH, gonadotrophs → FSH and LH). The corticotroph peptides are particularly clean: they all derive from *pre-pro-opiomelanocortin* (a 265-amino-acid precursor) which is cleaved into β-lipotropin, an N-terminal peptide, ACTH, α-MSH, CLIP, γ-lipotropin, and β-endorphin (Fig 12.4, [p.194]). The chapter's structural argument is that the apparent diversity of pituitary peptides is misleading; many are post-translational cleavage products of a single precursor, and the receptor specificities at the targets are what determine which one matters where. The glycoprotein hormones (TSH, FSH, LH) similarly share an identical α-subunit; the β-subunit is what confers biological specificity. Rand-style: the cell economises on synthesis by producing common precursors and letting the cleavage pattern (which is itself under regulation) determine the final hormonal mix.

The hypothalamic releasing/inhibiting hormones (Table 12.3, [p.196]) are seven small peptides: *vasopressin* and *CRH* stimulate ACTH; *LHRH/GnRH* stimulates LH and FSH; *TRH* stimulates TSH (and prolactin); *GHRH* stimulates GH; *somatostatin (GHIH)* inhibits GH, prolactin, and TSH; *dopamine* inhibits prolactin. Note that growth hormone is dually controlled (one stimulator + one inhibitor) and that prolactin's default state is suppression (dopamine tonically inhibiting), making prolactin the only anterior pituitary hormone whose hypothalamic regulation is primarily negative rather than positive. The textbook implication: removing pituitary stalk connection raises prolactin and drops everything else. [p.196]

The **posterior pituitary** secretes only two hormones, *oxytocin* and *vasopressin (ADH)*. They are nonapeptides differing in just two amino acids (positions 3 and 8), produced in the supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei, transported bound to neurophysin proteins down axons to the posterior lobe, and released by calcium-dependent exocytosis like ordinary neurotransmitters. Both have plasma half-lives of about a minute. [pp.197–198] Despite the structural near-identity, their functions are sharply distinct: vasopressin acts on V₁ receptors (vascular smooth muscle, raising IP₃ and intracellular Ca²⁺) and V₂ receptors (renal collecting ducts, raising cyclic AMP and water permeability), producing a concentrated urine and (at high concentrations) vasoconstriction. Oxytocin acts via G-protein-coupled receptors that raise intracellular Ca²⁺ in myoepithelial cells of the breast (milk ejection) and uterine smooth muscle (contraction). Rand's Ch 1 push-pull principle reappears here in a degenerate form: the body uses one molecular template (nonapeptide with a disulphide loop) and produces two effectors with diametrically different targets simply by swapping two residues.

**Vasopressin regulation** (Fig 12.8, [pp.198–199]) is the chapter's most complete worked feedback diagram. The integrating organ is the hypothalamus; the sensors are osmoreceptors (detecting plasma osmolality) plus baroreceptors and atrial volume receptors (detecting effective circulating volume via central venous pressure and arterial pressure). Stress is a third stimulator acting directly on the paraventricular nucleus. The effector is the kidney's collecting duct, where ADH inserts aquaporin channels to recover water. The negative feedback closes when increased reabsorption raises plasma volume and lowers osmolality, which inhibits both osmoreceptor firing and the volume-receptor feedback to the supraoptic nucleus. The disease consequences map directly: too much ADH (head trauma, ADH-secreting tumour) produces concentrated urine, water retention, hyponatraemia; too little (head injury, posterior pituitary damage) produces *diabetes insipidus* (the patient cannot concentrate urine even when osmolality rises and must drink continuously). A receptor-level deficit (nephrogenic diabetes insipidus) gives the same phenotype with intact ADH levels.

**Growth hormone** (12.3, [pp.199–201]) is the chapter's first anterior pituitary hormone discussed in depth. GH is a 191-amino-acid peptide synthesised in somatotrophs, secreted at ~1.4 mg/day in pulsatile bursts every 1–2 hours with marked elevations during deep sleep, and under dual hypothalamic control (GHRH stimulates, somatostatin inhibits). The chapter's clearest worldview move on GH is the split between its **direct metabolic effects** and its **indirect growth-promoting effects**, which act through different targets and at different time-scales.

Direct GH actions are essentially anti-insulin: GH decreases glucose uptake by muscle and adipose, increases hepatic glycogenolysis, promotes lipolysis in adipose tissue, and increases free fatty acid release. Net: hyperglycaemia and a respiratory quotient shift toward fat oxidation. GH is therefore *diabetogenic* in metabolic action. Simultaneously, GH stimulates amino acid uptake and protein synthesis in liver, muscle, and adipose tissue, producing positive nitrogen balance. The chapter's framing: GH is a fasting-state hormone in metabolic terms (conserves glucose, mobilises fat, builds protein) and a paediatric growth signal only because it also runs a second pathway.

Indirect GH actions are mediated by **IGF-1**, an insulin-like growth factor synthesised primarily by the liver under GH stimulation and acting locally on chondrocytes and many other cell types. IGF-1 stimulates cartilage mitosis at the epiphyseal plates, drives DNA synthesis and cell division in fibroblasts, and promotes the incorporation of sulphate into chondroitin (the cartilage matrix precursor). The growth-promoting effects of GH are therefore not direct: GH triggers IGF-1 release, IGF-1 acts on the cartilage, and cartilage converts to bone via endochondral ossification. Once the epiphyseal plates fuse at the end of adolescence, IGF-1 can no longer drive linear growth even though GH and IGF-1 both continue to circulate throughout adult life.

The chapter therefore stages GH as a clean example of the body's preference for indirect, factorable signalling. Rather than have GH itself act directly on every growth-relevant cell, the architecture relays the signal through IGF-1, which (a) amplifies it, (b) localises it to tissues that themselves synthesise IGF-1, and (c) decouples the growth signal from GH's metabolic effects so they can be regulated separately. The negative feedback loop closes when IGF-1 (and GH) both inhibit hypothalamic GHRH and stimulate somatostatin (Fig 12.9, [p.200]).

**Regulation and disorders of GH** follow the same architecture. The most potent metabolic stimulus for GH release is hypoglycaemia, which is the appropriate homeostatic response since GH's direct action is glucose-sparing (mobilising fat for ATP, conserving glucose for the CNS). Oral glucose suppresses GH. Other stimuli include prolonged fasting, exercise, stress, cold, hemorrhage, and amino acid loads. Negative feedback closes via two loops: GH itself inhibits hypothalamic GHRH and stimulates somatostatin (a short loop), and IGF-1 inhibits pituitary GH synthesis (a long loop). Disorders match the split. Excess GH in childhood (before epiphyseal fusion) produces *gigantism* with proportionate growth; excess GH in adulthood produces *acromegaly* (enlargement of hands, feet, jaw, tongue; organomegaly; thickened facial features; diabetic profile) without height increase, because the cartilage substrate is gone but the metabolic and soft-tissue effects persist. GH deficiency in childhood produces *pituitary dwarfism* (proportionate short stature, often accompanied by TSH and gonadotropin deficits if the pituitary defect is panhypopituitarism). Adult GH deficiency is metabolic rather than morphological and was historically untreated. [pp.201–203]

### 12.4 The thyroid gland

The thyroid is a discrete organ adhering to the trachea below the larynx, two flat lobes joined by an isthmus, total weight 10–20 g, with an exceptionally rich blood supply (5 mL/g/min). Its functional unit is the *follicle*: a colloid-filled cavity lined by follicular epithelial cells. The colloid is *thyroglobulin*, an iodinated glycoprotein. Scattered between or within the basement membrane are *parafollicular C-cells*, which secrete calcitonin (a separate system, see 12.6). The thyroid is anatomically unusual in that it stores several weeks' worth of hormone within the colloid, in contrast with most endocrine glands which store little. This explains why dietary iodine deficiency takes time to produce hypothyroidism and why anti-thyroid drugs take weeks to work. [pp.203–205]

The thyroid's chemistry is the most elaborate piece of biosynthesis in the chapter. Iodide is the only substance in the body containing iodine, and an adequate dietary iodine intake (≥75 μg/day) is essential. The synthesis sequence: (1) *iodide trapping*, by a Na⁺/I⁻ symporter on the basal follicular cell membrane, concentrating iodide 20–100-fold against an electrochemical gradient; the pump is stimulated by TSH and competitively inhibited by perchlorate and thiocyanate. (2) *Iodide oxidation*, by thyroid peroxidase at the apical membrane, using H₂O₂ as electron acceptor. (3) *Thyroglobulin synthesis* on the rough endoplasmic reticulum of the follicular cell, packaged into vesicles, exocytosed into the colloid lumen. (4) *Iodination* of tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin (positions 3 and 5) to form monoiodotyrosine (MIT) and diiodotyrosine (DIT). (5) *Coupling* of MIT + DIT → T₃ (3,5,3′-tri-iodothyronine) and DIT + DIT → T₄ (thyroxine), still attached to thyroglobulin and stored as colloid. The thyroid normally secretes about 80 μg of T₄ per day, 4 μg of T₃, and 2 μg of inactive reverse T₃ (rT₃). [pp.205–206]

Secretion requires hydrolysis. TSH stimulates the follicular cells to pinocytose colloid back across the apical membrane; lysosomes fuse with the colloid droplets and degrade thyroglobulin; MIT and DIT are deiodinated and the iodide recycled within the gland; T₃ and T₄ diffuse out the basolateral membrane into capillaries. Thyroglobulin itself is not normally released into the circulation; its appearance in plasma indicates gland inflammation or damage. TSH is therefore the master switch for nearly every step (iodide trapping, peroxidase, thyroglobulin synthesis, iodination, coupling, and secretion), and its absence produces rapid glandular atrophy. The hypothalamic side closes by classic negative feedback: T₃ and T₄ inhibit both TSH release and TRH-induced TSH responsiveness; estrogens raise TSH sensitivity to TRH; glucocorticoids suppress TSH. Cold stress stimulates the axis with a TSH peak followed by a T₃/T₄ peak over a few days. [pp.206–207]

Transport and metabolism are the chapter's clearest illustration of the chemistry-predicts-kinetics atom. T₃ and T₄ are tightly bound to plasma proteins (T₄: 75% to thyroxine-binding globulin, 15–20% to prealbumin, remainder to albumin; T₃ almost entirely to TBG). Free hormone is 0.5% of total T₃ and 0.05% of total T₄. T₄ binds with ten times the affinity of T₃, so its metabolic clearance is slower; half-life is 7 days for T₄ versus less than 1 day for T₃. T₄ is, in effect, the storage and transport form, a prohormone; peripheral deiodinases in liver and kidneys remove an iodine atom from T₄ to produce active T₃. A different deiodinase removes the inner-ring iodine to produce inactive *reverse T₃ (rT₃)*; this pathway increases in caloric restriction and severe illness, acting as an energy-conserving switch that downregulates the active hormone without changing TSH or T₄ levels. (This is why critically ill patients can have low T₃, normal-to-high rT₃, and a misleadingly normal TSH; "sick euthyroid syndrome.") [p.207]

The thyroid hormones bind nuclear receptors and act through gene transcription, so their effects are slow in onset (hours to days) and durable. They are general tissue growth factors and are required for normal development of the skeleton and CNS. Their three main physiological footprints are: (1) *calorigenesis*: most tissues raise oxygen consumption and heat production via increased mitochondrial mass and Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase stimulation. Notable exceptions with few thyroid receptors: brain, spleen, testes, uterus, anterior pituitary. The BMR rises within 24 hours of cold exposure. (2) *Synergism with catecholamines*: thyroid hormone potentiates the actions of epinephrine on heart rate, contractility, glycogenolysis, lipolysis, and thermogenesis, which is why β-blockers ameliorate many manifestations of hyperthyroidism. (3) *Metabolic effects on substrates*: low doses are hypoglycaemic (stimulating glycogen synthesis and glucose utilisation with insulin); high doses are glycogenolytic and lipolytic; chronically high levels cause net protein catabolism, especially in muscle (thyrotoxic myopathy). The chapter's framing is that thyroid hormone sets the gain on the entire metabolic and sympathetic machinery; it is not itself a primary effector of anything but rather a tonic potentiator of everything else. [pp.207–208]

Disorders match the dual role. *Hyperthyroidism* (most often *Graves' disease*, an autoimmune disorder in which thyroid-stimulating antibodies (TSAb) mimic TSH at the receptor) produces weight loss, sweating, heat intolerance, palpitations and atrial fibrillation, nervousness, raised BMR, fine tremor, and the characteristic *exophthalmos* (autoimmune extra-ocular muscle inflammation). Treatments: thioureylene drugs (block peroxidase), radioactive ¹³¹I (selectively destroys the most active follicles), or surgical resection. *Hypothyroidism* in adults (most often *Hashimoto's thyroiditis*, autoimmune destruction of follicular cells, or iodine deficiency producing *endemic goiter* through unrestrained TSH-driven hypertrophy) produces *myxedema*: puffy face, dry cold skin, alopecia, weight gain despite reduced appetite, constipation, lethargy, mental dullness, bradycardia. Treatment is oral T₄ replacement, taking advantage of its long half-life and slow conversion to T₃. *Cretinism* is severe congenital hypothyroidism (1 in 4,000 infants), producing irreversible mental retardation if untreated; the clinical practice of routine neonatal heel-prick screening for TSH on days 1–5 makes cretinism almost entirely preventable. The chapter therefore stages thyroid disease as the single most operationally informative endocrine pathology: the patient's symptoms are essentially a verbal version of the molecular pathway, top to bottom. [pp.208–210]

### 12.5 The adrenal glands

Each adrenal is two endocrine glands fused into one. The outer *adrenal cortex* (mesodermal origin) secretes steroid hormones and is essential to life: loss produces death within days unless replacement is given. The inner *adrenal medulla* (ectodermal, neural crest origin) secretes catecholamines and is not essential to life; it can be removed without major consequence. The chapter's structural point is that two completely different glands have been packaged together because they cooperate in the stress response. [pp.210–211]

The cortex has three concentric zones, each producing a different class of steroid: outer *zona glomerulosa* (~10%, mineralocorticoids), middle *zona fasciculata* (~75%, glucocorticoids), inner *zona reticularis* (sex steroids). The cells migrate inward through life, changing their secretory pattern as they move. All cortical steroids are synthesised from cholesterol via cytochrome P-450-mediated hydroxylations (Fig 12.22, [p.212]); the differences between aldosterone, cortisol, and adrenal androgens are which enzymes the cells of each zone happen to express. Once made, the steroids are not stored to any significant degree; they diffuse across the plasma membrane as fast as they are synthesised, so output is controlled entirely at the level of synthesis. [pp.211–212]

*Aldosterone* (zona glomerulosa, 0.1–0.4 μmol/day) is the principal mineralocorticoid. Its primary regulator is not ACTH but the renin-angiotensin system, which responds to plasma sodium and potassium and to renal perfusion (see Ch 17 of the textbook). Aldosterone acts at the distal nephron to reabsorb Na⁺ in exchange for K⁺. Failure produces hyperkalaemia, hyponatraemia, ECF volume collapse, and death; excess (*Conn's syndrome*, zona glomerulosa adenoma) produces Na⁺ retention, hypokalaemia, alkalosis, and hypertension via plasma volume expansion. *Cortisol* (zona fasciculata, 30–80 μmol/day) is the principal glucocorticoid. ~70–80% is bound to *transcortin* (corticosteroid-binding globulin), 15% to albumin, 5–10% free. Cortisol's actions oppose insulin: it facilitates conversion of protein to glycogen by mobilising amino acids from muscle to liver for gluconeogenesis, builds hepatic glycogen, raises plasma glucose, inhibits insulin-dependent glucose uptake, and stimulates lipolysis. It is also immunosuppressive, anti-inflammatory, anti-allergic, increases vascular tone (potentiating catecholamines), and acts on the CNS to produce euphoria. Fetal cortisol is essential for organ maturation, especially pulmonary surfactant production. *Adrenal sex steroids* (zona reticularis: dehydroepiandrosterone, androstenedione) are minor in normal adults; they become clinically significant in adrenal tumours or in *congenital adrenal hyperplasia* (a 21-hydroxylase enzyme defect that shunts steroid precursors into the androgen pathway). [pp.212–214]

The regulation of cortisol secretion is the chapter's cleanest worked example of a hypothalamo-pituitary-target negative feedback loop. CRH from the hypothalamus stimulates ACTH from the anterior pituitary; ACTH acts on adrenocortical cells via G-protein-coupled receptors and cAMP to drive cholesterol mobilisation and steroidogenesis. Cortisol then inhibits both CRH release and ACTH secretion (Fig 12.25, [p.215]). The architecture has a marked circadian rhythm: cortisol is at its minimum around 3 a.m., peaks at 6–8 a.m., and roughly half of the day's total output is delivered in the pre-dawn surge. Acute stress (physical or emotional) overrides the rhythm by directly stimulating CRH. Disorders therefore split cleanly. *Cushing's syndrome* (excess cortisol from pituitary or adrenal tumours, from ectopic ACTH-secreting tumours such as small-cell lung carcinoma, or from prolonged therapeutic steroid use) produces the characteristic "melon on toothpicks" habitus: central obesity with thin, atrophied limbs from protein wasting, moon face, buffalo hump, thin bruisable skin, hyperglycaemia, hypertension, and immunosuppression. *Addison's disease* (primary adrenocortical failure, often autoimmune) produces weakness, weight loss, hypoglycaemia, hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia, severe dehydration, hypotension, and a characteristic *hyperpigmentation* of skin and mucosa (because ACTH and α-MSH share a precursor; loss of cortisol feedback raises ACTH, which spills MSH activity into the skin). [pp.214–216]

### Adrenal medulla

The medulla is "an enlarged and specialised sympathetic ganglion." Its *chromaffin cells* are postganglionic sympathetic neurons that have lost their axons and secrete into the bloodstream instead. Preganglionic splanchnic nerve fibres release acetylcholine onto the chromaffin cells, which respond by exocytosing their stored catecholamines (80% epinephrine, 20% norepinephrine, together with dopamine β-hydroxylase, ATP, and opioid peptides like met-enkephalin). The biosynthetic pathway is tyrosine → DOPA → dopamine → norepinephrine → epinephrine; the final methylation step (phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase) is what distinguishes adrenal chromaffin cells from sympathetic nerve terminals elsewhere, which stop at norepinephrine. Plasma half-life of catecholamines is 1–3 minutes; inactivation is by reuptake into sympathetic terminals or enzymatic degradation (COMT, MAO). [pp.216–217]

The split actions of the two catecholamines map onto adrenoceptor subtypes (Table 12.4, [pp.216–217]). Epinephrine prefers β-receptors: glycogenolysis (β₂), lipolysis (β₃), calorigenesis (β₁), insulin and glucagon secretion (β₂), tachycardia and increased contractility (β₁), bronchodilation (β₂), and arteriolar dilation in skeletal muscle (β₂). Norepinephrine prefers α and β₁: gluconeogenesis (α₁), inhibition of insulin (α₂), vasoconstriction in non-muscle vascular beds (α₁) producing the rise in diastolic pressure, bronchoconstriction (α₁), and increased gastrointestinal sphincter tone (α₁). Clinically, the receptor map is the prescription: β-blockers like atenolol drop heart rate and blood pressure; β₂-agonists like salbutamol dilate the asthmatic airway; α-blockers treat the catecholamine surges of *pheochromocytoma* (a chromaffin-cell tumour producing episodic hypertension, hyperglycaemia, anxiety, tremor, palpitations, surgically curable). Adrenal medullary failure has essentially no clinical consequence, because the sympathetic nervous system covers the same functions; the gland's only physiological role is amplification during stress. [pp.216–217]

### 12.6 Calcium, phosphate, vitamin D, PTH, calcitonin

The chapter's last regulatory system is the calcium-phosphate axis, which differs from the others in that it has three competing hormones acting on three target tissues (bone, kidney, gut), with no central pituitary-driven loop. Of the body's 1 kg of calcium, 99% is in bone as *hydroxyapatite*; only 10 g circulates between bone, ECF, and intracellular compartments. Plasma total calcium is 2.3–2.4 mmol/L (half ionised at 1.4 mmol/L, half bound to albumin and globulins, the rest complexed with phosphate). Intracellular free calcium is held at ~0.1 μmol/L, more than 10,000× lower than the extracellular pool; the steep gradient is what makes calcium useful as a second messenger but lethal if the boundary breaks. Hypocalcaemia below ~1.5 mmol/L produces *tetany*: increased neural and skeletal-muscle excitability, carpopedal spasm, Chvostek's sign (facial twitch on tapping the facial nerve), and potentially convulsions. Hypercalcaemia produces fatigue, constipation, kidney stones, CNS depression. The body therefore needs a regulatory system that responds in minutes to keep ionised calcium inside a narrow corridor, and the three hormones do it. [pp.218–219]

*Vitamin D* is a steroid hormone produced in two organs from a single substrate. Skin: 7-dehydrocholesterol → cholecalciferol (D₃) under UV light. Liver: D₃ → 25-hydroxycholecalciferol. Kidney: 25-hydroxy → either 1,25-dihydroxycholecalciferol (*calcitriol*, active) or 24,25-dihydroxycholecalciferol (inactive). The kidney's choice is regulated; low plasma calcium and high PTH drive the active 1α-hydroxylation, which is why the system is fundamentally a calcium-controlled hormonal cascade rather than a vitamin in the strict sense. Calcitriol acts via nuclear receptors to upregulate calcium-binding proteins in the intestinal mucosa, stimulating active intestinal absorption of calcium and phosphate. It also acts on bone to facilitate remodelling (stimulating both osteoblast and osteoclast activity, perhaps by enabling calcium availability for matrix calcification). Deficiency produces *rickets* in children (skeletal deformities, bowing of weight-bearing bones from uncalcified matrix) and *osteomalacia* in adults (reduced bone density, fracture susceptibility, bone pain). Excess produces *vitamin D intoxication* (hypercalcaemia, nausea, vomiting, soft-tissue calcification). [pp.219–220]

*Parathyroid hormone* is secreted by the chief cells of the four parathyroid glands embedded in the posterior thyroid. PTH is an 84-amino-acid peptide cleaved from a 115-aa pre-pro-PTH precursor, with a plasma half-life of just 5 minutes; this short half-life is what allows minute-to-minute calcium regulation. The chief cells carry a calcium-sensing receptor on their membrane; low plasma calcium drives PTH secretion within seconds. PTH acts on three targets to raise plasma calcium: (1) *Bone*: short-term rapid release of calcium from the readily-exchangeable surface pool; long-term stimulation of osteoclastic resorption of stable bone (high PTH levels). (2) *Kidney*: increases calcium reabsorption in distal tubules; decreases phosphate reabsorption in proximal tubules (so PTH paradoxically lowers plasma phosphate, which itself raises free ionised calcium by reducing complex formation). (3) *Gut* (indirectly): stimulates renal 1α-hydroxylation of vitamin D to calcitriol, which then enhances intestinal calcium absorption. The integrated PTH effect is therefore: raise plasma Ca²⁺ from all three sources (bone, kidney, gut), and lower plasma phosphate via the renal action. Disorders: *primary hyperparathyroidism* (parathyroid adenoma or ectopic PTH-like secretion from lung tumours) produces hypercalcaemia, hypophosphataemia, kidney stones, bone demineralisation ("brown tumours"), and the cluster of symptoms remembered as "stones, bones, abdominal groans, psychic moans." *Hypoparathyroidism* (usually accidental surgical excision during thyroid surgery) produces hypocalcaemia, treated with calcium plus vitamin D metabolites. [pp.220–222]

*Calcitonin*, secreted by the thyroid parafollicular C-cells in response to elevated plasma calcium, inhibits osteoclast activity and stimulates renal calcium excretion. Plasma PTH and calcitonin therefore form an inverse pair across the normal calcium range (Fig 12.32, [p.221]). Despite this textbook symmetry, calcitonin's physiological significance in humans is uncertain: patients with calcitonin-secreting tumours (medullary thyroid carcinoma) have normal plasma calcium, and patients with surgical loss of the thyroid (and therefore loss of calcitonin) handle calcium normally on replacement of T₄ alone. Calcitonin's main clinical use is therapeutic, in *Paget's disease* of bone (excessive bone turnover) and *hypercalcaemia of malignancy*. The chapter's structural note is that the textbook push-pull design (PTH up, calcitonin down) does not necessarily mean both arms are operationally weighted equally in humans; the asymmetry is itself a finding. [pp.221–222]

Other hormones modulate the calcium-phosphate axis as a side effect of their primary metabolic actions: growth hormone and IGF-1 (anabolic on bone), glucocorticoids (catabolic; chronic Cushing's produces osteoporosis), thyroid hormones (high T₃/T₄ → catabolic), and especially estrogens. Estrogens inhibit PTH-mediated bone resorption and stimulate osteoblast activity; their fall at menopause produces postmenopausal osteoporosis (vertebral compression fractures, wrist and hip fractures). Estrogen replacement reduces the rate of bone loss; bisphosphonates and modern alternatives target the same pathway via osteoclast inhibition. [p.222]

### 12.7 Gastrointestinal tract hormones (brief)

The GI tract is the largest endocrine organ by total cell mass. Major hormones: *cholecystokinin (CCK)* from intestinal I-cells in response to dietary fat, stimulating gallbladder contraction; *secretin* and *vasoactive intestinal polypeptide (VIP)* stimulating pancreatic alkaline secretion; *gastric inhibitory peptide (GIP)* inhibiting gastric acid; *gastrin* from G-cells of the gastric glands, stimulating gastric acid secretion. *Insulin* and *glucagon* (the pancreatic islet hormones controlling plasma glucose) are discussed in detail in Ch 27 of the textbook. The chapter's last note is therefore that the boundary the chapter began with (classical vs. non-classical endocrine organs) has effectively dissolved by the end: the gut is doing the work of the pituitary, the heart is doing the work of the kidney, and adipose tissue is signalling to the brain. The endocrine system is everywhere in the body, and the chapter's enumeration of "principal glands" is a teaching convenience rather than an anatomical description.
