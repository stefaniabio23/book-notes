### Prologue


# Memories, Dreams, Reflections, Prologue

Jung opens by refusing the autobiographical contract:

> My life is a story of the self-realization of the unconscious. [p.3]

That single opening sentence does several jobs at once. It names the book's subject as the unconscious, not the man. It makes a [personal myth] claim before any reader can object. It pre-empts the scientific frame Jung's clinical peers would expect. "I cannot employ the language of science to trace this process of growth in myself, for I cannot experience myself as a scientific problem" [p.3]. The standards by which clinical biography would judge him do not apply, because the inner life he wants to record has no scientific apparatus suited to it.

The substitution Jung proposes is myth. "What we are to our inward vision, and what man appears to be [sub specie aeternitatis], can only be expressed by way of myth" [p.3]. Myth, in his frame, is more individual than science: "Science works with concepts of averages which are far too general to do justice to the subjective variety of an individual life." Jung's truth standard for what follows is psychic fidelity. He makes that explicit on the same page: "Whether or not the stories are 'true' is not the problem. The only question is whether what I tell is *my* fable, *my* truth."

Most autobiographies bury their epistemology and let the reader infer it from tone. Jung stages his in three pages before any chronology begins. The reader who came for Freud-Jung gossip, dates, marriages, or the Burghölzli years rendered as chronicle has been warned. Jung's book treats outer events as the visible portion of something else.

That something else is the rhizome:

> Life has always seemed to me like a plant that lives on its rhizome. Its true life is invisible, hidden in the rhizome. The part that appears above ground lasts only a single summer. Then it withers away, an ephemeral apparition. [...] What we see is the blossom, which passes. The rhizome remains. [p.4]

The [rhizome metaphor] is the structural skeleton of the whole book. Every chapter that follows is built on it. External events (school, university, career, marriage, war) get short summary treatment. Dreams, visions, and the [confrontation with the unconscious] get extended chapters of their own. Jung is announcing the proportions of his life as he sees them, and those proportions are inverted from a conventional memoir.

> The only events in my life worth telling are those when the imperishable world irrupted into this transitory one. [p.4]

He calls these inner experiences his [prima materia], the alchemical raw material from which his scientific work was "crystallized." The placement matters. Jung plants an alchemical term on page four, before any reader has been prepared for how heavily his late thought depends on alchemical symbolism. By the final chapters this language is everywhere; here it is the seed.

Jung's last move in the Prologue disqualifies external biography in advance: "Recollection of the outward events of my life has largely faded or disappeared. But my encounters with the 'other' reality, my bouts with the unconscious, are indelibly engraved upon my memory" [p.5]. The sentence works as both warning and permission. Warning: do not expect chronology. Permission: when Jung devotes thirty pages to a single dream and three to his marriage, this is by design.

The Prologue teaches the reader the right pace and aperture for the rest of the book. Read the dream chapters slowly. Read the career chapters as connective tissue. The centre of gravity is below the surface, and Jung has spent his three opening pages making that the contract.
