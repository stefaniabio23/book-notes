## The Origins of Efficiency

by Brian Potter

*Stripe Press, 2025. Hardback, 13-digit ISBN 978-1-953953-53-7.*

*Notes I took while reading this book to further my own learning. If you enjoy these notes, please [buy the book](https://press.stripe.com/the-origins-of-efficiency).*

*Published review: [stephanierebecca.com/books/the-origins-of-efficiency](https://stephanierebecca.com/books/the-origins-of-efficiency/)*

### Introduction

- The book's question: where do improvements in production efficiency come from? [Production efficiency] meaning producing goods and services in less time, with less labour, using fewer resources.
- Potter's core claim is that production efficiency is the *engine* of civilization, not a corporate-housekeeping concern. Falling production costs is the mechanism behind nearly every consequential change in human material life.
- The standard story of penicillin is misleading. Fleming's 1928 discovery and the 1940 Florey/Chain/Heatley isolation were necessary but not sufficient. Penicillin became a miracle drug because it was scaled, not because it was found.
- During the first human trial, an Oxford police officer with a *staphylococcus* infection improved on penicillin, then relapsed and died because the lab's annual yield was exhausted mid-treatment.
- Four production breakthroughs unlocked penicillin: corn steep liquor as growth medium (30× yield), submerged fermentation, a high-yield mold strain found on a moldy cantaloupe, and X-ray-induced mutations producing *Wisconsin Q-176* (100× Fleming's original).
- Penicillin volume went from "not enough for one patient" in 1941 to enough for the Allied armed forces by September 1943. Production rose from 80M units/month in 1943 to 18B units/month in early 1944.
- A 600mg vial of penicillin cost $200 in 1943 and $1.30 in 1952. By 1982 production hit 12,000 tons/year, 2,400× the 1945 volume, with inflation-adjusted price down 1,000×.
- Antibiotics, downstream of these production gains, extended average human lifespan by roughly 23 years and cut US bacterial-illness deaths by ~70% between 1936 and 1952.
- The pattern generalises. In 1500 CE roughly 75% of European populations worked in agriculture; in the modern US it's 1.2%. US wheat output is 11× its 1866 level, corn 20×, on a fraction of the land per bushel.
- Famine deaths globally fell ~96% between the 1870-1970 period and the post-1990 period. The 1960s–80s [Green Revolution] alone cut food prices 35-65% and raised caloric intake 11-13% in developing countries.
- Pre-industrial textile economics were brutal: a Viking sail required ~60 miles of thread (385 days spinning + 600 days wool prep). A Roman toga needed 25 miles of thread and 900 hours of spinning labour.
- England in 1770 had 1–1.5 million spinners out of a 4 million workforce. Modern spinning mills produce ~750× the per-person output of a preindustrial hand-spinner. Mechanisation cut British cotton-thread production cost >90% between 1784 and 1832.
- Gutenberg's 1440s system combined a screw press, cast metal type using a lead/tin/antimony alloy he invented, and a lampblack-and-varnish ink also his. Output: 200-250 sheets/hour vs days-per-page hand copying.
- 18th-century powered presses pushed printing to 1,000+ sheets/hour, roughly *10,000× hand-copying*. Book production rose ~50× between 1300s and 1500s; per-page cost fell to 1/50 of medieval levels by 1700s England.
- European literacy moved from ~12% in late 1400s to 31% by 1700s. Great Britain went from 5% to >50% over the same window. Cheaper text was the precondition for mass literacy.
- [Total factor productivity], the output per unit of all inputs combined, rose >7× in US agriculture and >9× in US manufacturing between 1800 and 2000. Annual hours worked per person fell ~50% in Western countries over the last 150 years.
- Wealth accumulation explains part of the rise. A modern US farm uses ¼ the labour of a 1948 farm but 2× the equipment. But TFP isolates the residual — efficiency gains beyond what extra capital inputs alone can explain.
- "New technology improves efficiency" is both too broad and too narrow as an answer. Too broad because it doesn't distinguish technologies that cut cost from those that don't. Too narrow because cost reductions also happen without new technology.
- Counterexamples of technologies that *failed* to make things cheaper:
  - *Nuclear power*. A 1 GW coal plant burns 7,000 tons/day of coal; a 1 GW nuclear plant uses 0.07 tons/day of uranium pellets. Yet US nuclear cost has risen ~10× since the 1970s.
  - [Factory-built housing]. Decades of attempts to industrialise homebuilding; no sustained cost reduction. Chapter 10 details the autopsy.
  - *Space shuttle*. Launch costs were 10× the Saturn V's. Space launch costs in 1970-2010 consistently exceeded 1960s levels until SpaceX, which used different launch technology entirely.
  - [Solar PV] and the [electric arc furnace]. Both invented in the late 1800s; took decades to a century to become cost-competitive with incumbents.
- The mirror failure: cost reductions in the *absence* of new technology. 1970s Japanese carmakers outproduced US plants on older machinery. Design for manufacturing, value engineering, and lean methods all cut cost organisationally rather than technologically.
- Potter's framing move: there are a limited number of ways to intervene in a production process to make it cheaper. The book enumerates them.
- Chapter map. Ch 1 — what a production process *is* and where intervention points sit. Ch 2 — new production methods. Ch 3 — cheaper inputs and product redesign. Ch 4 — economies of scale. Ch 5 — removing steps. Ch 6 — variability and control.
- Continuing. Ch 7 — [learning curves] and how improvements accumulate. Ch 8 — bundles, chains, and feedback loops where one improvement triggers others. Ch 9 — [continuous processes] as the convergent endpoint that efficient industries tend toward. Ch 10 — failures to improve. Conclusion — the future.
- The closing stake: many goods (housing, healthcare, education, space travel) remain expensive. Mastering production-efficiency mechanisms is how those costs come down. The book is implicitly a [progress agenda], not a descriptive history.

### Ch 1: What Is a Production Process?

- A [production process] is a series of steps that transforms input materials incrementally into a finished product. Each step changes the material and passes it to the next.
- Outputs of one process are inputs to the next. Bulb blanks fed Edison's light-bulb assembly. Potassium carbonate fed the bulb-blank chemical mix. Civilisation is processes chained into processes.
- Everyday objects mask huge production complexity. Thwaites's *Toaster Project*: 404 parts, >100 different materials in one $7 toaster. Leonard Read's *I, Pencil* essay: the full input chain involves millions of people across the world.
- A 19th-century watch factory boasted 3,700 distinct operations per watch. A 1940s Cadillac required nearly 60,000 separate operations.
- **Bulb-blank manufacture is the chapter's running example.** Sand, lead, and potassium carbonate (plus trace niter, arsenic, manganese oxide) melted in a furnace. A [gaffer] gathered glass on a hollow iron tube, blew the blob into a mold, cut it free. First Corning blanks for Edison (1880) cost ~3¢ each.
- Services follow the same model. A house cleaner sequences rooms, applies labour and supplies, transforms a dirty house into a clean one. The framework does not require a factory.
- **Five factors of any production process.**
- **(1) Transformation method.** The physical or chemical change at each step. Has its own substructure (a step is itself a series of motions). The model is necessarily simplified; [scientific management] studied this all the way down to individual worker motions.
- **(2) Production rate.** Output per unit time. Each step has its own rate. The bulb-blank crucible was filled weekly but blown daily, so rates differed by orders of magnitude across the same process. Three workers produced ~150 bulbs/hour, ~1,500/day with molds.
- **(3) Inputs and outputs (with costs).** Direct material inputs (chemicals, fuel), labour inputs (worker time), and indirect inputs (factory rent, equipment depreciation, administration, insurance). All are apportioned across output to compute per-unit cost.
- *Efficiency improvement is defined precisely* as anything that reduces per-unit cost.
- **(4) Buffer size / work in process.** Material that has entered the process but is not currently being worked on. If crucibles are filled weekly, ~half a week of molten glass sits idle on average. Inventory between steps is paid for but not yet sold.
- **(5) Variability of output.** Some bulbs are slightly thicker, slightly different composition, slightly different shape. Some fail outright (a cracked crucible, a dropped bulb). Characterised crudely as *yield* (fraction of inputs becoming acceptable outputs) or more finely as distributions over key properties.
- The model is a simplification. Factors fluctuate over time (variation rises with new workers or end-of-shift fatigue). Steps influence each other (furnace temperature affects blow-mold quality). Product form and production method are coupled (changing one usually requires changing the other). These complications get unpacked across the book.
- **Six ways to improve a process**, mapping onto the five factors plus one meta-move:
  1. Change the [transformation method]. Free-hand glass-rolling at Corning (165 bulbs first day) replaced by mold blowing (150 bulbs/hr per worker).
  2. Increase the [production rate] to capture economies of scale. Glass furnaces ran 24 hours because cold-starting damaged crucibles and took >24 hours to reheat. Continuous operation spread fixed costs over more output.
  3. Reduce [variability] in the process. Lower furnace-temperature variation produced more glass within acceptable tolerance, lifting yield.
  4. Lower **input costs.** Mold-blowing replaced free-hand blowing and reduced the skill required, allowing less expensive labour.
  5. Reduce [work in process] by shrinking buffers. Half-size crucibles filled twice weekly cut average idle glass by 50%.
  6. *Delete an entire step.* Buying pre-mixed glass powder skips the in-house mixing operation and removes all its associated costs.
- **The ideal extreme.** A [continuous flow process]: no buffers, no variability, no wasted steps, cheapest possible inputs with no wasted outputs, maximum scale, minimum-input transformation method. Inputs flow in, products flow out, every step synchronised.
- The highway analogy. City driving is stop-start at lights. Highway driving is consistent flow. Most real processes are between the two; the goal is to push toward the highway.
- **Bulb manufacturing's century-long evolution toward continuous flow** is the chapter's worked example.
- 1891: 7.5M US bulbs/year, mostly manual production. 1907: a 60W bulb cost $1.75 (~$54 in 2022 dollars). 1912: Corning's Empire E semiautomatic blowing machine, 400 bulbs/hr (2× the manual mold method).
- Fully automatic Westlake machines arrived next. 1921: 1,000 bulbs/hr. 1930s improved Westlake: 5,000 bulbs/hr. Mechanism still imitated hand-blowing.
- 1926: Corning [ribbon machine] introduced a different mechanism entirely. Molten glass poured continuously onto a conveyor, sagged through holes into bowl shapes, captured by a second conveyor's mold, blown from above, released as finished bulbs. First version: 16,000 bulbs/hr. By 1930: 40,000/hr. By the early 1980s, fewer than 15 ribbon machines made the world's supply at ~120,000 bulbs/hr each (33 bulbs/second).
- The ribbon machine is the chapter's central case of [form follows efficiency]. It does not look like a faster hand-blower. It is a fundamentally different physical mechanism that suits continuous flow.
- Parallel improvements happened across the assembly side. Filament mounting, tube attachment, sealing, lead-in wire insertion all automated. Vacuum pumps went from 5-hour Edison-era cycles to fast automatic units.
- *Work cells.* From 1921, Westinghouse and others rearranged separate machines into synchronised groups so one machine fed directly into the next. WIP fell sharply, storage requirements dropped, output per worker nearly doubled.
- The result. 60W bulb cost $1.75 in 1907 → 10¢ in 1942. Bulb *efficiency* (lumens/watt) nearly doubled over the same period. Combined with cheaper electricity, *cost per lumen fell 98.5% between 1882 and 1942*.
- The chapter's promise. The five-factor framework, plus the "delete a step" meta-move, plus the continuous-flow ideal, explain efficiency improvements in any industry. The next nine chapters unpack one factor each.

### Ch 2: New Processes

- Production method is the load-bearing variable. The choice of transformation determines rate, input costs, variability, and buffer size. Change the method and everything else moves with it.
- Three generations of nail-making efficiency. Hand-forged (Roman through 1800), cut-nail (machine cuts body from sheet, 30,000/day by the 1870s), wire-nail (wire fed in, sized, headed, hundreds of thousands/day by 1900). Each generation cut price ~50%, of which roughly half is the process change.
- Nails were ~0.4% of US GDP in 1810, about as load-bearing as computers today. Colonial buildings were sometimes burned down to recover them.
- Technological progress follows an *S curve*. Slow start with the mechanism poorly understood; rapid middle as a [dominant design] emerges and incremental improvements stack; plateau at the natural performance limit. A successor technology starts its own S curve at a higher ceiling.
- Edison's incandescent lamp took eight decades of unsuccessful attempts before the right combination landed. Thin carburised cotton filament, Sprengel mercury pumps for high vacuum, a double seal, and a high-resistance design matched to centralised distribution.
- Incandescent [luminous efficiency] rose roughly 8× over 50 years through filament chemistry alone (carburised bamboo, flashed carbon, metallic carbon, osmium, tantalum, ductile tungsten). A modern incandescent is ~17 lumens/watt; LEDs reach a theoretical ~300.
- Aircraft speed went from 6.8 mph (Wright Flyer 1903) to 466 mph (Heinkel He 100 1939) on piston engines, then plateaued near ~550 mph. Jets opened a new S curve to >2,000 mph.
- Steam-engine S curve. Newcomen (<5% thermal efficiency), Watt (3× Newcomen, separate condenser killed the cylinder-reheating loss), compound engines, steam turbines. Each successor used a different mechanism with a higher ceiling.
- [Multiple performance axes] complicate single-S thinking. Bulb adoption is luminous efficiency *and* colour, cost, lifespan, dimmability. Fluorescents won commercial settings on efficiency but lost residential to incandescents on colour quality.
- *Innovator's Dilemma* pattern. New technology often arrives worse on the mainstream axis, better on a niche axis that creates its own market. Hydraulic excavators started at 1/20 the scoop capacity of cable rigs but won residential trenching, a use case that hadn't existed at scale.
- Military funding bridges the precommercial valley. Semiconductors, jet aircraft, nuclear power, interchangeable parts, and numerically controlled machine tools all required defence subsidy before commercial viability.
- **Method, design, and function are coupled.** A new method is rarely a drop-in substitute. Cut nails could not be clinched; wire nails were weaker. Ford's Model T was *all black* because pre-1923 coloured paint took weeks and 14 coats. Duco pyroxylin (1923) opened the spectrum but only at duller finishes.
- [Design for Manufacturing (DFM)] reverses the dependency. Tomatoes were bred tougher so machines could harvest them. Hybrid corn was bred for stiff stalks and uniform height. Cars were standardised to suit the assembly line.
- **Production technology is unportable.** Moving a method requires moving the operational skill that runs it. British workers could not run Samuel Colt's London factory. DuPont's first indigo run came out green until they poached German chemists.
- Within-industry productivity dispersion is enormous. Top-quartile plants are typically 65% more productive than average, sometimes more than 2× the average. Same equipment, different operational depth.
- TSMC's Oregon fab has not, after 25 years, matched its Taiwan facilities. GM never replicated NUMMI elsewhere. [Tacit knowledge] is the load-bearing obstacle.
- Intel's [Copy Exactly] technology-transfer rule requires new fabs to duplicate existing fabs down to paint colour and supplier. The response to environmental sensitivity so high that early fab yields were affected by phase of the moon and bathroom-handwashing residues.
- Bessemer steel only worked because Henry Bessemer happened to use *low-phosphorus* iron without realising it. High-phosphorus ores gave bad steel; he had to refund licensees. Pilkington's float-glass tank lining had the correct microscopic composition by accident.
- [Performance ceilings] are hard to predict. The gas mantle let gas-lighting nearly bankrupt Edison. Wind turbines kept getting bigger past predicted limits. Optical lithography kept advancing past predicted ceilings.
- Successor technologies do not always arrive. Wire nails, ribbon machines, Hall-Héroult aluminium, and Haber-Bosch ammonia are all 100+ years old and still dominant. Some processes simply approach their natural floor and stop.
- [Mechanisation] replaces manual labour with machines. Manual labour is the original general-purpose technology, a 7-DoF arm with 30+ grasp types and full sensorium. Hard to beat across the entire envelope. Easy to beat on any single axis.
- Machines rarely reproduce human motion. Wheels replace legs. Reciprocating blades replace hammering. Printing presses do not write script. Different mechanism, similar output.
- Mechanisation usually requires designing the product to suit the machine. Lightbulb filaments became short rigid coils when assembly was mechanised because long draped wires were hard for machines to manipulate.
- Mechanisation proceeds in stages. Light-bulb assembly went from 200/hr with 10 manual workers (1920) to 300/hr with 5 (1925) to 700/hr with 7 (1936) to 1,350/hr with 4 (1955) to ~2,000/hr today.
- Highest automation lives where products are uniform and high-volume. Garment manufacturing still requires humans to piece fabric and feed sewing machines because deformable materials remain hard to model and manipulate.
- Mechanisation rarely eliminates labour fully. Humans oversee, repair, and respond to anomalies machines cannot. The 1942 textile-worker's *chalk your bobbins* trick (rubbing chalk on wooden bobbins to prevent fibre snags) was a piece of tacit operational skill no engineering report had captured.

### Ch 3: Reducing Input Costs

- A production process is an input bundle (materials, components, energy, labour, equipment, facilities) becoming a product. Lowering the bundle's cost, even without changing the production technology, is the lever.
- Four levers plus one corollary. Redesign the product, redesign the process, change the organisational structure (make vs buy), change the location, plus raise the value of by-products.
- **Product redesign.** McMaster-Carr lists >50,000 screw types. A car's part combinations exceed the atoms in the universe. The design space is vast and cheaper variants almost always exist.
- Newcomen-engine cylinders moved from brass to cast iron mid-18th century. Cannons went bronze to cast iron in the 15th-16th centuries. Light bulbs eliminated platinum progressively, with the last bit replaced in 1912 by an iron-copper-brass composite matching glass's thermal expansion.
- Henry Ford allegedly specified Model A battery shipping boxes so the wood pieces would become the driver's-side floorboards. The brake and starter holes were the vent holes. The screws holding the box together bolted the floorboards down.
- [Value engineering] (Lawrence Miles at GE, 1947) asks of every component: what does it do, what does it cost, what else does the same job for less? Spread to RCA, Westinghouse, IBM. Georgia DOT study: $23 to $300 saved per $1 spent on value engineering.
- [Design for Manufacturing and Assembly (DFMA)] (Boothroyd and Dewhurst, 1970s-80s) extended value engineering by also designing for ease of assembly. The number-of-parts count is load-bearing. Combining parts saves more in assembly operations than it costs in fabrication complexity.
- Manufacturability and assembly are often in tension. Producibility guidelines reward simple-to-make parts but produce designs with more parts, which adds assembly cost. DFMA balances both sides explicitly.
- **Process redesign.** [Operations research] tools (linear programming, the simplex method) cut paper-roll trim loss. Indian single-line railway analysis found train crossings grew faster than n², so combining trains lifted daily distance >30% with fewer scheduled trains.
- *Single-Minute Exchange of Die (SMED)* (Shigeo Shingo, 1950s-60s). Reduced die changes from hours to <10 minutes. Key move: convert *internal* setups (machine stopped) to *external* setups (assembly while machine is running). Generalises to all changeovers.
- [Route design] in pharma. An early antipsychotic-drug route used $2,300/kg raw materials, mostly an expensive aldehyde at 65% yield. An alternative route cut raw-material cost ~50%. Route choice can shift total drug cost by orders of magnitude.
- [Work simplification] / scientific management: ask what, why, how, who, where, when of every step. Motor-rewinding case shortened a single operation from 30 minutes to 30-60 seconds with specialised tooling.
- **Make-versus-buy.** Coase's [transaction costs] frame. Commodity input with many suppliers → buy. Specialised input with supplier-specific equipment or knowledge → make.
- Ford's Rouge plant (75,000 workers in 1928) is the canonical vertical-integration case. Owned forests, sawmills, iron mines, coal mines, railroads, glass plants, a tire plant, a paper mill. Even sold cement made from blast-furnace slag and *Kingsford* charcoal from sawmill waste.
- Rouge integration cut a Model T from $680 (1911) to $260 (1924), a 75% real reduction. But the plant was Model-T-specific. Switching to the Model A in 1927 required a six-month shutdown costing $100M ($1.7B in 2022 dollars) plus $250M in lost sales.
- Chrysler's *under*-integration helped during the Depression. Lower fixed costs, suppliers desperate to sell cheap. Chrysler's share went from 6% (1927) to 25% (1933).
- By 1965, GM was 48% integrated by wholesale value, Ford 40%, Chrysler 27%. Foreign labour and outsourcing reversed the trend later. Average US firm size shrank 20% between 1970-1997.
- **Location.** Labour-intensive industries chase cheap labour. Garments: Manhattan, Brooklyn, NJ, New England, US South, overseas. Nike footwear: Japan, Taiwan/Korea, China, Vietnam/Indonesia. Shipbuilding: UK, Japan, South Korea, China.
- Energy-intensive industries chase cheap electricity. Iceland and Norway smelt disproportionate aluminium because power is ~30% cheaper than in the US. Iceland smelts nearly as much aluminium as the US with 1/900 the population.
- Pre-rail, water transport was an order of magnitude cheaper than land. 19th-century factories clustered in port cities. Chauncey Jerome's New Haven clocks cost less than half what European clocks did because the port made inputs cheap.
- **Value of outputs.** Coal-tar synthetic dyes. Blast-furnace slag (originally a "nuisance of little intrinsic value") became a $470M/year US cement-alternative market by 2019. Paraffin candles dominated the 19th-century candle industry off coal-distillation waste.
- The [Leblanc soda-ash process] emitted hydrogen chloride. By the 1860s, the bleaching-powder by-product became more valuable than the soda ash itself. Gasoline was a kerosene-production waste until cars made it the valuable product.
- **Trade-offs.** Methods sit on a high-fixed-cost vs high-marginal-cost spectrum. Glass bottles: machine-blown for wine, hand-blown for art. Injection moulding vs 3D printing: low unit cost at high volume vs no setup cost at low volume.
- [Coupling] between steps means choices propagate. Abraham Darby's early-1700s coke-fuelled blast furnace produced higher-silicon iron, which cooled faster, which allowed unheated (cheaper) moulds for cast-iron pots.
- **Explore-exploit dynamic.** Empire State Building's cladding-support beam was a novel arrangement of well-understood materials (exploit). Edison's filament search and 1930s flush riveting in aircraft were explorations of partially-known territory.
- [Adjacent possible]: combinations of known elements that have not yet been tried. Flush riveting used well-known materials but required trial-and-error to learn how hole size, dimple angle, die pressure, and metal composition interacted.
- The lowest-cost recipe today is not the lowest-cost recipe tomorrow. Basic oxygen process displaced open hearth in 1960s steel; electric arc displaced basic oxygen by the 2020s. Wire nails displaced cut nails once Bessemer steel made the wire cheap. Gasoline became the prize once cars existed.
- **Tesla's Giga Press case study.** Replaced hundreds of stamped-steel parts in the Model Y underbody with two large aluminium castings. Estimates: labour cost down 65%, factory floor down ~50%, total body cost down 20-40%. Cast aluminium minimum thickness ~2mm (vs 0.7mm steel) and demands smooth flow paths, no blind walls, no undercuts.
- Tesla used [binder jetting] (sand 3D printing) to make test moulds in hours, cutting die-validation time up to 75%. Developed a novel high-strength aluminium alloy that skipped heat treatment entirely.
- The Tesla story is the chapter's thesis in miniature. The redesign was made possible by simultaneous improvements in casting technology, additive manufacturing for mould iteration, and alloy chemistry. *Process change is rarely a single technological advance.*

### Ch 4: Production Rate and Economies of Scale

- [Economies of scale]: production costs fall as production volume rises. Modelled as C ∝ x^b where b < 1 gives increasing returns to scale.
- Modern blast furnaces produce ~3M tons/year of pig iron. 16th-century English furnaces produced 250-300 tons/year. A 10,000× scale increase, mostly in the late 19th and 20th centuries.
- US pig-iron prices fell ~40% in real terms 1870-1910, largely driven by rising furnace scale.
- Three large empirical studies (1967, 1992, manufacturing-wide) all found economies of scale in 60-80% of plants surveyed. The biggest plants typically had labour costs 60-90% below the smallest, with overall costs 2× to 7× higher in the smallest.
- **Five mechanisms produce economies of scale**, plus a sixth that enables them.
- **(1) Fixed cost spreading.** Spread one-time costs (land, equipment, setup, structure) over more units. The 1923 mechanised blast-furnace rebuild doubled output with the same labour, halving per-unit labour cost.
- [Economies of scope] (variety) and [economies of speed] (throughput per unit time) are sub-types of fixed-cost spreading.
- Fixed-cost effects appear at every level. Factory, machine, individual setup, individual tool grab. Division of labour partly works by spreading the cost of "grab the tool" over more units of work.
- **(2) Geometric scaling.** Surface area rises with r², volume with r³. Equipment cost scales like surface area, capacity like volume, so larger equipment is cheaper per unit of capacity. The [six-tenths rule] (scaling exponent ≈ 0.6) is the chemical-plant default, giving ~25% per-unit cost reduction per doubling of capacity.
- Container ships: a 12,000-TEU ship has roughly half the per-container cost of a 1,500-TEU ship. Both steel and fuel scale geometrically.
- Scaling up requires redesigning equipment. Nuclear plants needed completely new emergency-cooling systems once they got large enough that molten fuel could melt through the containment building. Wind turbines and blast furnaces both had to be scaled gradually because behaviour changed nonlinearly with size.
- **(3) Statistical scaling.** Larger production volume damps random variation. Peak electric demand averages out across more customers. Spare-parts inventory rises sublinearly with machine count. Safety stock rises with √(demand).
- **(4) Influence scaling** (pecuniary effects). Large buyers get bulk discounts, force suppliers to bear adaptation costs, and dictate quality standards. Apple's supplier code of conduct runs 206 pages.
- **(5) Learning curve effects.** Wind-generated electricity costs fell ~18% per doubling of installed capacity in California 1980-1994. Covered fully in Ch 7.
- [Network effects] are not economies of scale but enable them. Every new airline city adds routes to every existing city, multiplying revenue passenger miles and justifying larger aircraft, which are themselves cheaper per seat-mile.
- **Diseconomies of scale.** Coordination cost rises with n² potential communication links per new employee. One acquisition study found firms 50% more likely to have civil-lawsuit judgments after scaling via M&A, attributed to [diseconomies of managing].
- Demand-side diseconomies: exhausting nearby inputs. US steel imported iron ore at 2% of domestic production in 1920 and over 30% at the 1970s peak. [Beneficiation] of low-grade ores can cost up to 3× the mining cost itself.
- Geometric diseconomies: tall buildings. Bending moment scales with height². Deflection with height⁴. Each new floor adds weight to all floors below, more elevators, stricter fire requirements. Skyscrapers have an [economic ceiling height] beyond which marginal cost exceeds marginal benefit.
- Statistical diseconomies: larger machines have more failure points. A 500 MW power unit and ten 50 MW units have similar total tubing, but one tube failure costs 10× more availability in the big plant.
- **L-shaped cost curves.** Empirically common: costs fall to an elbow then flatten, rather than the U-shaped curves predicted by classical theory. [Minimum efficient scale] (MES) is the elbow, the plant size needed to be competitive.
- Aircraft manufacturing's MES is large enough that the global market supports only Boeing and Airbus. Machine-tool MES is <1% of market and has hundreds of producers.
- Many efficiency-improvement systems *act through* scale. Division of labour benefits from learning effects and reduced setup costs as workers specialise. Ford spent $2.7B developing the 1990s Taurus, roughly 100,000× the cost of one car, viable only because of expected unit volume.
- **Mass production requires mass consumption.** Cast-iron stoves rose from 25,000/year (1830) to 2.1M (1870) in the US because the Erie Canal (1825) and the rail network (40 to 50,000 miles by 1870) made shipping the heavy product economic.
- Container shipping after 1970 enabled the global supply chain. Each country specialises in one input, captures scale, ships globally. Barbie dolls of Japanese hair, Taiwanese plastic, American colourant, Chinese assembly.
- [Semiconductor fabless model] is a downstream consequence. EUV-capable fabs cost billions and are only viable for the very largest in-house users (Intel) or as foundries (TSMC, GlobalFoundries) serving many fabless designers (AMD, Nvidia).
- **False economies of scale.** Conventional logic was to run a machine for months without changeover to amortise long setups. SMED disproved that. Reducing the setup time itself is a strictly better move than spreading it over more units.
- *Lower the water level to expose the rocks.* High inventory in conventional mass production masks process problems. Toyota deliberately runs lean inventories to make breakdowns immediately visible.
- **False diseconomies of scale.** 1995 wind-power conventional wisdom held that turbine cost scaled with diameter³ and energy with diameter², capping economic size at ~40m rotors. Modern turbines exceed 120m onshore, 150m offshore. Wind-power costs fell ~90% between 1984 and 2022 partly because the small-turbine wisdom was wrong.
- The constraint people assumed was binding (turbine mass) was not. Modern wind-turbine blades weigh ~90% less than 1990s equivalents.
- **Bottlenecks.** A process's throughput equals its slowest step. [Theory of constraints] (Goldratt) and [critical path method] (DuPont, 1950s) formalise the idea. In project management, "typically only about 10% of the jobs in large projects are critical."
- Truck-bumper plant raised output by reallocating non-welding tasks away from welders. Truck-lamp plant raised throughput by prioritising injection-moulding tooling collection. The lever is moving work *off* the bottleneck, not adding capacity everywhere.

### Ch 5: Removing a Step

- The cheapest step is no step. Cutting an unnecessary step removes 100% of its inputs plus an entire scaffolding of support operations.
- **Value-adding vs non-value-adding.** [Value-adding] steps perform the physical transformation a customer pays for (cutting a nail body, forming a head). [Non-value-adding] steps are scaffolding (carrying iron from storage, repairing a jam, inspecting).
- The customer is the arbiter of value. Standard Toyota framing. Most operations fail the test.
- Liker on a truck-bracket attachment: 14 steps, only 3 (position bracket, place bolts, tighten) are value-adding. Most business processes are ~10% value-added work.
- Womack: total time to make an aluminium soda can from bauxite is ~319 days. Actual value-adding time is ~3 hours, or 0.04% of total. Liker found a steel-nut process at 0.008%.
- [Removing non-value-adding steps] is usually easier than removing value-adding ones because the product itself does not change. Removing a value-adding step typically requires redesigning the product or moving the function elsewhere.
- Ford's assembly line moved the work to the worker. Three principles: place tools and men in operation sequence so each part travels the least distance; use slides and gravity to deliver finished parts to the next worker; use sliding assembly lines.
- [Scientific management] (Taylor) led to [job analysis]: divide each job into operations, identify the one best way. Taylor ran 30,000+ metal-cutting experiments and published *On the Art of Cutting Metals* (1906).
- Frank Gilbreth's bricklaying study cut ~70% of motions and tripled the rate. Trained as a contractor, scaled scaffold height to bricks, used edge-up brick frames so the bricklayer never had to flip or examine bricks.
- The Gilbreths' [therbligs] were 17 standard micro-motions. *Effective* therbligs (reach, move, grasp, assemble) vs *ineffective* therbligs (search, plan, inspect, hold). The taxonomy predates the value-add / non-value-add lean vocabulary by decades.
- [Predetermined time systems] (work factor) measured motions in 1/10,000-of-a-minute units, removing stopwatch judgement entirely. Used at RCA TV assembly to set output rates.
- [Work simplification] (Mogensen, 1930s+). Analyse every step, eliminate the unnecessary, simplify the rest. A pea-cannery process went from 23 steps to 16 with a $51 investment saving $464/year ($631 saving $5,792 in 2023 dollars).
- By 1954, 129 of 207 large US companies had formal work-simplification programs, including Sears, Honeywell, and DuPont. Adopted by US Navy and Air Force.
- **Toyota's eight wastes (muda).** Overproduction, waiting, unnecessary transport, overprocessing, excess inventory, unnecessary movement, defects, plus unused employee creativity (added later). Five are non-value-adding steps directly. Two (overproduction, excess inventory) are inventory waste.
- Lean practitioners report 15-25% cost reduction per round of waste removal.
- [Poka-yoke] (mistake proofing). Devices that prevent or instantly flag errors. Toyota averaged 12 poka-yokes per machine in the 1990s. Example: two angled bars across a stem-tightener chute classify each part as too thick, good, or too thin and divert each automatically.
- [Autonomation] (jidoka): automation with a human touch. Machines stop autonomously when they detect an error. One worker can tend many machines without producing batches of bad parts when one fails.
- [Five whys] (Ohno): trace any problem to its root cause by asking "why" five times. Catches design issues that survive surface-level fixes.
- [Removing value-adding steps] requires changing the product or process. DFMA's main benefit is reducing assembly operations by combining parts.
- [Float glass] case. Pilkington (1950s-60s) replaced pouring + grinding + polishing of plate glass with floating molten glass on molten tin. Eliminated hundreds of feet of grinding/polishing line, used 50% less energy, 80% less labour, generated 15-25% less scrap. Float glass became cheaper than the previous *low*-quality sheet glass; the two product categories collapsed into one.
- [Interchangeable parts] initially added steps (manual filing and adjusting to make parts uniform) until machine tools became precise enough to produce uniformity directly. After that, the same idea removed all the fitting work and made repair easy.
- The line between value-adding and non-value-adding is fuzzy. Some apparently value-adding operations duplicate effort elsewhere or provide features the customer does not value ([overprocessing]).
- A nickel-silver dish manufacturer was polishing the undersides. Customers did not care. Eliminated.
- Tesla discovered the fibreglass mats it was installing on the battery pack had no measurable impact on noise. Eliminated the mats and the robots installing them.
- Elon Musk: "Try and delete part of the process." If you do not have to add at least 10% of removed steps back later, you did not delete enough.

### Ch 6: Variability, Knowledge, and Control

- Every production process has variation: human, machine, material, environmental, and from incompletely understood effects.
- Injection-moulded parts typically hold ±0.005 inches. Structural steel plates may vary 12% from nominal thickness. Bulb-blowing varies in glass thickness and shape.
- *Two costs of variation.* [Destructive variation]: output outside tolerance, scrapped or reworked. [Misalignment]: adjacent steps running at different rates require buffers (inventory or capacity), both of which add cost.
- [Taguchi loss function]: any deviation from the ideal value causes cost, even within tolerance. Ford transaxles with more dimensional variation produced more warranty claims. Sony saw the same in TVs.
- A simulation of a pin factory with mean 10 sec/step and σ = 3 sec produces large inventory buffers between machines after 100,000 steps. Every buffer carries cost.
- **Two strategies for handling variation.** Reduce or control it, or make the product or process *robust* to it.
- [Reducing variability] requires both knowledge of the factors and control over them. Historically knowledge was tacit and craft-based. Wrought-iron [puddling] could not be learned from books.
- The 18th-19th-century shift from craft secrecy to open knowledge enabled accumulation: Diderot's *Encyclopédie*, engineering-drawing conventions, the metric system, books on furniture, windmills, and mechanical movements.
- Frederick Taylor's 30,000+ metal-cutting experiments produced quantitative formulas relating tool diameter, cut depth, tool life, and cutting speed.
- Measurement instruments expanded controllable variables. Wedgwood's pyrometer for kilns. Herschel's Venturi meter for liquid flow. Early-20th-century factories had thousands of recording instruments displaying internal state.
- **Shewhart's distinction.** [Assignable cause variation] has a traceable source and can be eliminated. [Chance cause variation] is the residual once assignable causes are removed and forms a statistically stable distribution.
- *Statistical process control (SPC)* uses X-bar (sample mean) and R (sample range) charts with ±3σ bounds. Data outside bounds or patterns within bounds reveal an assignable cause to hunt.
- One electrical-switch manufacturer reduced magnetic-intensity variation by fixing sources sequentially: operator over-adjustment, loose fixtures, premature parts removal (added a cooling timer), then machine vibration (relocated motors).
- New technology pushes back the [consistency frontier]. Maritz's horizontal cannon-boring engine (1715). Wilkinson's precision cylinders for Watt's steam engine. Synthetic dyes replacing batch-variable natural dyes.
- [Shielding] breaks the causal path between variation source and process. Factories enclose against weather. Air conditioning was *invented* as humidity control for textile mills, bakeries, and chocolate makers.
- **Feedback control.** Thermostats compensate without needing to predict every disturbance. Watt's *flyball governor* (>75,000 in use on English engines by 1868) closed steam valves when engine speed rose and opened them when speed fell.
- 1745 Edmund Lee's [fantail] automatically rotated a windmill into the wind. 1787 Thomas Mead's tentering mechanism kept the millstone gap constant against wind-speed variation.
- Manual operators are flexible controllers but limited by speed and accuracy. Automatic clay-kiln temperature control cut waste from 20% (manual) to a small fraction.
- Herbert Henry Dow ranked automatic control with the steam engine and interchangeable parts as foundational industrial advances.
- **Toyota Production System as a variation-reduction case.** Goal is just-in-time, one-piece flow. Reduce inventory buffers, surface every disruption immediately, diagnose with the five whys.
- [Heijunka] (production levelling) keeps daily production rate constant. Demand variation is absorbed by pricing adjustments rather than inventory.
- [Kanban] caps work-in-process between every pair of process steps. The previous step can only produce if a card has come back from the next step.
- Toyota emphasises [genchi genbutsu] ("go and see"), quality-control circles (9 implemented suggestions per worker per year by 2005), and job rotation for cross-training ([shojinka], flexible staffing).
- **Robustness, not elimination.** Relaxing unnecessary tolerances saved 98% on one valve shaft. Tight tolerances are expensive. If the strictness was not functionally needed, it is pure waste.
- [Selective assembly]: bin oversized pistons with oversized cylinders. Ford used it for the Liberty aircraft engine in WWI.
- [Variability pooling]. The [Jones mixer] pooled output of many blast furnaces to even out silicon variation feeding Bessemer converters. Benetton dyed sweaters late, after demand signals. HP attached country-specific power supplies at the distributor, not the factory.
- [Taguchi methods]: design experiments to find process-parameter combinations *least sensitive* to noise. One ceramic-tile factory cut defect rate from 30% to <1% by raising limestone content from 1% to 5%, immunising the tiles against the kiln's uneven temperature.
- [Pull systems] (kanban) are more robust to small inventory-cap errors than [push systems] (scheduled release), where errors compound into runaway WIP or chronic underutilisation.
- **Heuristic.** Will reducing variation reduce cost? Ask: does the reduction add or remove operations on balance? Early-1800s interchangeable parts *added* operations (manual filing). By the early 20th century, machine-tool precision made interchangeability *subtract* operations.
- Variability has good applications. Selective breeding of mutant penicillin strains lifted yield 4,000× over Fleming's original. Selective breeding of teosinte over 9,000 years made modern corn. Hirschmann's forging case: a worker accidentally over-heated dies; the company discovered dies could be made smaller and used longer.
- **Lean processes are fragile to unexpected shocks.** Covid-19 cost automakers ~$110B in lost production from chip shortages. Toyota, the inventor of just-in-time, was *least* affected because it stockpiled six months of semiconductors after the 2011 Fukushima supply lesson.
- Toyota's choice to hold chip inventory is what they called a *classic lean solution*. Lean is not "zero inventory." It is "no waste, then resilience against variation you cannot control."

### Ch 7: Learning Curves

- [Wright's law] (Theodore Wright, 1936): production cost falls in proportion to cumulative output. Each doubling of total production produces the same percentage cost reduction.
- Modelled as y = ax^−b on a log-log plot, giving a straight line. Slope b is the [progress rate].
- Solar PV: $100/watt in 1975 to $0.26/watt in 2021 (inflation-adjusted). ~23% cost reduction per doubling of cumulative production.
- Boeing B-17: first units took >140,000 labour hours. By unit 6,000, <20,000 labour hours. Achieved partly while *worker skill decreased*: experienced workers were drafted; replacements were less skilled. The skill lived in the process, not the people.
- Researchers have documented >100 learning curves. Ford Model T, Tesla Model 3, LEDs, fighter jets, chemicals, steel, petroleum, electricity. [Moore's law] (time-based) is in fact better predicted by Wright's law (volume-based) given exponential volume growth.
- **Three effect types behind the curve.** (1) Learning effects, the kind the name implies. (2) Indirect effects from changed incentives. (3) Artifact effects, spurious correlation.
- **Learning effects.** Skill can be in individuals (Lowell mill workers 4× more productive at 12 months than at 1 month). More often, skill is embedded in the process itself.
- Boeing reorganised B-17 assembly into smaller subassemblies, wrote step-by-step production illustrations, and implemented an early just-in-time system at a B-24 factory after finding parts travelled up to a mile between workstations 50 feet apart.
- Process ramp-up takes months. Semiconductor fabs can take years to exceed 50% yield. No process ever reaches 100% yield, so the search for improvement never ends.
- Equipment learns too. An aluminium fabricator doubled an electric-arc furnace's output through accumulated operating adjustments. Petroleum cracking units gradually exceeded design output by exploiting safety margins.
- Bigger units have to be designed gradually. Wind turbines, blast furnaces, steam turbines all scale in steps because behaviour changes nonlinearly with size.
- **Indirect effects.** Larger orders forced fibreglass makers to invent [continuous pultrusion] machines. Higher demand justifies higher R&D spend, scale economies, and adoption of high-fixed-cost methods.
- **Artifact effects.** Reverse causality (price falls drive demand which drives cumulative volume) and simple time-based improvement that happens to correlate with volume.
- WWII production data lets researchers separate learning from time because volume varied non-monotonically. Experience effects explained 67% of labour-hour reductions and 40% of cost reductions across 152 vehicles and 523 products.
- [Horndal effect]: 2%/year productivity gain at a Swedish ironworks 1935-1950 *with no added capital*. Suggests pure learning, without new equipment, is modest compared to learning paired with technological investment.
- **The learning curve is the S curve's back half.** Reframe cost-per-unit as units-per-cost and orientation flips to match the S-shape. The flat early stage of an S curve is hidden because learning curves only count cumulative *production*, not pre-production R&D.
- **Modelled.** A technology with many subtechnologies, each capable of random improvement, with a performance floor, naturally produces learning-curve-shaped cost decline.
- Connected components produce shallower learning curves. Single-family homes have this problem. Changing wall framing changes insulation, plumbing, electrical routing, so structural insulated panels (SIPs) have not succeeded despite paper attractiveness.
- [Reverse salients]: a strongly-connected component that fails to improve becomes the bottleneck and dominates total cost.
- **Manual-intensive processes have steeper learning curves than machine-intensive processes** because rearranging people is easier than redesigning machines. Implication: design products to be modular and standardised so separate components can advance independently.
- **Disruption resets the curve.** A new apparel style at one factory reset costs to a higher level. Boeing 707: labour-hours per plane jumped at each new customer due to custom requirements. Intel's [Copy Exactly] (cited again here) was designed to prevent this.
- Forgetting happens without explicit changes. A TV-component factory that produced in intermittent 1-3 week bursts lost efficiency vs continuous operation. [Toe-up phenomenon]: costs rise at the tail end of a run.
- [Negative learning]. US nuclear plant construction costs *rose* through the 1970s-80s on continuously changing safety regulations, lost supply chain, and lawsuit-driven schedule slips. Lockheed's TriStar never achieved a learning curve and lost the company billions.
- **Solar PV vs titanium.** Both have ~23% learning rate. PV cumulative production rose 300,000× since 1975; titanium rose only 5-10×. PV is now ubiquitous; titanium remains niche. The Kroll process for titanium has not been replaced since the 1930s, capping titanium's market expansion. Learning curves describe *opportunities*, not *guarantees*.
- [Continuous improvement] (Liker, lean) requires stability first. "If the process is shifting from here to there, then any improvement will just be one more variation that is occasionally used and mostly ignored. One must standardize, and thus stabilize, the process before continuous improvements can be made."
- US Navy deliberately schedules nuclear-submarine construction continuously to preserve skill. The UK lost this skill during a fallow period and paid heavily on resumption.
- Learning curves use log-log plots that conceal real-world dispersion. In solar PV 1976-2016, costs differed from the curve's predicted value by up to 150% in any given year. The curve smooths over genuinely chaotic local conditions.
- Most cost-vs-volume relationships with diminishing returns will fit *some* learning curve. The shape is partly an artefact of plotting choices. The curve is most useful as a map and a comparative tool, not as a forecast.

### Ch 8: Bundles, Chains, and Feedback Loops

- Most efficiency-improvement mechanisms do not appear in isolation. A single process change typically triggers improvements along multiple axes ([bundles]) and unlocks subsequent improvements ([chains]).
- Ford's assembly line was one change that did all of: cut non-value-adding motions, sped each operation (magneto assembly 20 min to 13 min initially, then to 5 min after refinement), spread fixed costs by raising volume, synchronised worker pace (fast workers slowed, slow workers sped), and eliminated inter-station inventory.
- Machine-made [interchangeable parts] similarly bundled: removed fitting and filing operations, reduced assembly-time variance, enabled less-skilled labour, and made special-purpose jigs and fixtures economical.
- **Bundles.** A production-method change is the most common source of bundled improvements because the method touches every other variable (rate, inputs, variation, buffers).
- [Removing a step] bundles. Each step has its own input cost, scaffolding, and failure rate, so removing the step also removes failure probability. Pharma chemists pursue routes with the fewest steps because each step has <100% yield and the losses multiply through.
- [Reducing work in process] bundles inventory-cost reductions with all the supporting-operation cost reductions (storage, transport, tracking, inspection).
- [Standardisation] is a bundle. Greater scale on the standard part, higher yield, fewer custom-design costs, lower training cost.
- **Chains.** One improvement unlocks the next. Automatic cement-kiln control (1950s) made much larger kilns feasible by removing the human-control bottleneck. New scale was downstream of a control improvement.
- Bottleneck removal unlocks downstream improvements. Reducing variation at step n removes buffer-and-fitting scaffolding at step n+1.
- *Lower the water level to expose the rocks*. Cut WIP to surface problems the buffer was hiding. One birdfeeder manufacturer noticed glue-gun reliability problems only after reducing inventory caps.
- *Monden's TPS diagram* makes the chain explicit. Three chains feed into greater profit. (1) Higher quality via autonomation, leading to higher sales and lower service. (2) Lower workforce cost via cross-trained workers and flexible staffing. (3) Inventory reduction via JIT, kanban, and SMED.
- TPS must be implemented whole. SMED plus kanban plus cross-training plus machine cells plus autonomation plus heijunka. Piecemeal adoption fails.
- **Virtuous circles.** Economies of scale are the canonical example. Lower cost, more sales, more volume, more scale economies.
- The flip side is a death spiral. Early-2000s landline phone companies: fewer customers, higher per-customer cost on the fixed network, higher prices, even fewer customers.
- **Ford Model T as the canonical chain.** Vanadium steel and precision machine tools made interchangeable parts feasible. Interchangeable parts made the assembly line feasible. The assembly line made special-purpose tooling economical. Special-purpose tooling made unskilled-labour operation feasible. Cheap labour made low prices possible. Low prices made high volume possible. High volume made the next round of all of the above economical.
- Model T (1908) pushed tolerances to ±1/64 inch. "The rock upon which mass production of the Model T was based." Engines did not need pre-installation testing because the parts were correct by construction. Maintenance cost ~$100/year vs $1,500 for typical contemporary cars.
- Casting innovations. Engine block cast as a single piece (vs four separates). Crankcase, rear-axle housing, transmission housing as stamped steel (cheaper than cast steel). Transmission housing later moved to cast aluminium.
- Ford shipped knocked-down kits to local assembly plants. A freight car held 26 disassembled cars vs 3-4 complete ones. Eventually all Model Ts were painted black.
- Highland Park (1910): electric motors instead of belt drives, gravity chutes through floor holes, machine tools packed close in operation sequence. ~15,000 machine tools by 1914, in constant rearrangement.
- The forged-bracket case shows the math. Eliminating one bracket from 200,000 cars (1914) saved 3,300 hours of installation, 1.2M screws, 1.2M nuts, 1.6M drilled holes, and ~$1,200 on screws alone. Tiny per-car improvements multiplied by huge volumes are enormous.
- First moving assembly line, April 1913, flywheel magneto: 20 min to 13 min, then 5 min after refinement. By August, chassis line dropped 12.5 hr to 6 hr to 3 hr, reaching 93 min by April 1914. Engine line went 594 min to 226 min.
- Failed experiments were tolerated. A $180,000 piston-making machine ($5M in 2023 dollars) was "thrown into the yard" after repeated failures and replaced with simple lathes. High-volume production made experimentation cheap relative to expected gains.
- **Two themes underneath everything.** Accumulation, where many small improvements compound. Scale, where scale enables accumulation and accumulation enables more scale. Modern semiconductors and AI both follow this pattern. Trillions of transistors per second, models trained on the entire text corpus, made possible by scale at every link in the chain.

### Ch 9: Continuous Processes

- Production processes that improve along the various efficiency axes converge on a single end state: [continuous processes], where inputs are continuously transformed into outputs with no buffers, no rework, no waiting between steps.
- Papermaking case. Pre-1799: cut rags, ferment, beat into pulp, vat, hand-mold (the [vatman]), batch-press, dry in lofts, size with animal glue. Discrete steps with large WIP between each.
- 1799 [fourdrinier] mechanised molding and pressing. Pulp-water flowed onto a shaking wire belt; water drained; rollers squeezed paper into a continuous sheet rolled at the end. Steam drying added 1820, automatic sizing 1830.
- By 1850, machine-made paper cost 1/8 of handmade. Fourdriniers went from 100 ft/min in 1867 to >700 ft/min by 1919.
- **Continuous vs batch.** A [batch process] accumulates work and processes a fixed quantity at once. A heat-treating furnace doing 100 parts at a time is a batch step.
- *Natural gas* example. Pipeline (Rockies Express, 1,679 miles): continuous. LNG tanker: batch, with ~600× volume reduction by liquefaction.
- **Why continuous tends to beat batch.** Higher WIP in batch even at equal output. A 10-piece batch process averages 7.5 pieces in WIP vs 2 in one-piece flow, with cycle time 12 sec vs 2 sec in the pin-factory simulation.
- Batch processes amplify error impact. One bad batch ruins 100 parts. Continuous processes catch the error on piece #1.
- Schonberger comparison: Japanese factories making the same product with the same equipment were *one-third the size* of US factories because they did not need WIP storage. One-third of the building cost saved.
- **When batch wins.** Geometric scaling (bigger furnace, bigger ship). Flexibility (a *job shop* uses general-purpose equipment for many products; an LNG tanker can dock anywhere; a pipeline cannot).
- Chemical industry pattern: small-volume specialty chemicals on batch processes, high-volume commodity chemicals on continuous. Coffee roasters: specialty roasters batch, commercial roasters use continuous belts.
- Container shipping is the counter-example to convergence. Ships keep growing (now 20,000+ TEU). Slow steaming has *lengthened* cycle times. Scale economies overwhelmed the WIP cost.
- **Hybrids.** Precast concrete parking garages combine continuous erection on site (cranes set pieces as trucks arrive) with batched production at the factory (long casting beds, weeks of inventory before construction starts). Each step optimised separately.
- **Toyota** generalised continuous-process logic to *small-batch, high-variety* production. Cells, SMED, cross-trained workers achieved continuous flow without losing flexibility.
- *Group technology.* Workstations grouped into cells handling families of similar parts. Japanese shipbuilders adopted it post-WWII; combined with statistical quality control, captured a large share of the global shipbuilding market.
- **Historical sequence.** Oliver Evans's automatic flour mill in Delaware, 1785, with 6 workers mostly closing barrels. Bonsack cigarette machine 1881, 30 of which supplied all US demand by 1885. Diamond Match's continuous machines 1870s. Ford's assembly line 1913.
- [Mass production] was Ford's term for continuous production of *complex* assembled goods (thousands of parts) at high volume. Generalised continuous-process logic from simple commodities and bulk goods.
- **The corollary.** A continuous process often marks the *end of an S curve*. No successor technology appears. Fourdriniers still make paper 200+ years later. Hall-Héroult still smelts aluminium. Haber-Bosch still makes ammonia. Solvay still makes soda. Wire-nail machines still make nails.
- Small numbers of large-volume facilities can supply entire economies. <15 ribbon machines made the world's incandescent bulb blanks in the 1980s. 20% of US infant-formula supply came from a single plant whose 2022 shutdown caused a national shortage.
- Continuous processes are sometimes substituted, but almost always by other continuous processes. Cut-nail machines were replaced by wire-nail machines; both are continuous.

### Ch 10: Failures to Improve

- Not every process gets cheaper. Some get more expensive in real terms. Construction, medical care, and college tuition outpace inflation. Manufactured goods, electronics, and clothing have fallen.
- Two reasons a process fails to improve. (1) [Blocked paths]: process improvements cannot happen due to technical, political, or market limitations. (2) [Added burdens]: regulation and labour costs accumulate faster than improvements arrive.
- **Technical limitations.** Titanium is fundamentally expensive. It dissipates heat slowly, so cutting tools wear fast. It absorbs impurities at high temperatures, so up to 95% of aerospace billet is machined away as waste. The [Kroll process] is batch and requires an inert atmosphere; no continuous replacement has emerged in 80+ years.
- **Political limitations.** Building codes are typically *prescriptive* (specify how to do it), not *performance-based* (specify what it must achieve). Prescriptive codes lock in specific methods. Car crash tests are performance-based, which is why Tesla's aluminium-casting body redesign was permitted without regulatory change.
- Stakeholder opposition is centuries old. Plasterers' unions lobbied to ban drywall. Plumbers' unions tried to ban PVC. Dockworkers resisted shipping containers. 1561 Nuremberg suppressed Hans Spaichl's slide-rest lathe. 1579 Danzig's ribbon-loom inventor was reportedly drowned by city-council order.
- **Market limitations.** Low-volume products cannot reach economies of scale or fall down learning curves. EUV lithography machines: 50/year produced globally, $200M each, no path to mass production.
- *Solar PV history* shows what jump-starting can do. Military and satellite demand led to German feed-in tariffs, which led to utility-scale deployment. Subsidies make sense when the only obstacle is volume, not technical infeasibility.
- **Process stability is a precondition for improvement.** Medical care is intrinsically variable (every patient differs), which resists automation and streamlining, so costs rise. Same logic: a car repair is proportionally far more expensive than a new car. The new car is a high-volume repetitive process; the repair is bespoke.
- **Added burdens.** Regulation accumulates. Catalytic converters and airbags added $6,000-$7,000 to a car's cost between the 1960s and 2010s. Coal-power scrubbers (1970s air-quality rules) made coal more expensive. US manufacturers spend ~$17,000 per employee per year on environmental compliance.
- *Baumol's cost disease.* Wages rise in productive sectors. Wages also rise in unproductive sectors because labour pools are shared. A string quartet today plays the same notes, in the same time, with the same labour as a century ago, but commands much higher relative pay.
- **Construction case study.** Real US construction costs rose faster than inflation in almost every decade since 1900. A 1946 California subdivision built for $5.69/sqft ($92 in 2022) is now $178/sqft on average.
- Labour productivity in US construction is flat. 85 labour-hours per sqft in 1962. 86.5 in 2022. The economy-wide gain over the same period was ~200%. Same pattern in Germany, France, Belgium, Sweden, Norway.
- **Why construction has not industrialised.** Operation Breakthrough (1969 HUD program), Lustron, Stirling Homex, Katerra all failed or went bankrupt. Toyota Housing Corp (1975+) has built thousands of prefab homes in Japan but at ~$200/sqft, roughly 2× US average. Toyota is a small player in Japanese homebuilding despite Toyota literally writing the book on lean.
- [Dollar density] is the technical obstacle. A $10 million shipment of iPhones fits in a 20-foot container. The same volume holds only $30,000 of single-family home material. Low dollar density makes transport disproportionately expensive, limits factory catchment areas to roughly a day's drive, and caps potential market size per factory.
- [Manufactured (mobile) homes] are the closest exception. ~50% cheaper than site-built in the US. But the gap shrinks as homes get larger and more conventional. Multi-section "real-looking" prefab homes are ~70-80% of site-built cost. Significant, but nothing like the >90% reductions when other industries moved to factories.
- **Construction input variability.** ~20,000 US permitting jurisdictions, each with discretion. Sites vary in soil, climate, infrastructure, neighbours, and code interpretation. Even within one factory, modules are not uniform.
- **Material substitution is constrained by low dollar density.** Drywall is hated by prefab builders (heavy, fragile, time-consuming) but substitutes are far more expensive. Cold-formed steel studs are more uniform than dimensional lumber but 14% more expensive (HUD study).
- **Cyclical industry.** Construction shipments dropped >50% in 2008 and never recovered to pre-crisis levels. Factories have higher overheads than on-site builders, so they survive downturns less well. Selection pressure favours the craft model.
- **Low design effort.** Building design costs ~5% of build cost. Car design costs ~1000× the unit. With minimal value-engineering, building elements stay oversized.
- [Bricklaying robots] have been attempted for a century. Limited success. Mortar handling is hard, each brick needs a position map, setup and teardown costs are high. Construction materials and tasks evolved for human ergonomics; replicating them mechanically is hard without rebuilding the entire method.
- **Vertical integration is rare in construction.** Lennar and DR Horton subcontract almost everything. License requirements prevent flexible-staffing-style reallocation (drywallers cannot do electrical work). Variability and small per-region markets eliminate the scale advantage of integration.
- **Construction methods stay on the same S curve.** Lacking a high-volume repetitive process to absorb risk, builders prefer incremental tweaks. Cost of a failed novel method is asymmetric (fat right tail; little upside).
- 2005 Harvard Joint Center study: largest US homebuilders (>10,000 homes/year) had slightly *higher* costs per square foot than small builders. Influence-effect scale economies are small relative to the absent geometric and fixed-cost economies.
- **Drug development matches the pattern.** Cost of developing a new drug rose 14× in real terms between the 1970s and 2010s. Physician and nurse incomes tripled in real terms 1960-2016. [Route design] has lowered manufacturing cost of individual drugs by orders of magnitude, but trial cost and labour cost are uncovered by that improvement.
- **The unfinished agenda.** Mass-producible, repetitive goods have benefited from efficiency improvements. Goods that are bespoke, location-tied, or biologically variable have not. Housing, healthcare, education. Pushing efficiency further into those resistant areas is the work that has not been done.
- The chapter's framing for the book as a whole. Efficiency improvements created enormous wealth, but the gains were concentrated in a narrow slice of the economy. The technology to repeat the trick elsewhere is partially known. The political and organisational work to deploy it is not finished.

### Conclusion: The Future of Production

- The five mechanisms recur as the book's spine. Technological improvement (Industrial Revolution textiles, steam engines), input-cost reduction (DFMA as table stakes), [economies of scale] (Second Industrial Revolution in America), removing steps (lean methods originating in Toyota), and variability reduction (Japanese conquest of shipbuilding). They interact via bundles, chains, and virtuous circles, which is why no single mechanism explains efficiency on its own.
- Scale looms largest. It is both an efficiency mechanism in its own right and the *gating* mechanism that unlocks all others by amortising the fixed costs of value engineering, DFMA, automation, and continuous-process machinery. The Corning ribbon machine could only justify itself across millions of bulbs/day.
- The deeper claim. Improving a process has historically required *repeating* it. Without repetition, scale economies vanish, variability-control loses its target, and mechanisation has nothing to copy. This is why medical care, car repair, and construction stay expensive: skilled labour adapts to high variability instead of repeating.
- The book's open question is precisely how to escape this coupling: get the benefits of a continuous process *without* needing to repeat that process over and over again. Some couplings are unavoidable (geometric scaling). Others are contingent on the historical fact that continuous processes used dedicated equipment.
- **Flexible equipment + information processing is the proposed escape route.** Two requirements: (1) physical equipment with enough degrees of freedom to take many actions or combine few actions into many outputs; (2) an information-processing layer that can break a desired output into a sequence of achievable steps given a variable environment.
- The [printing press to inkjet printer] analogy carries the argument. Rotary presses ran continuously but required high-volume runs to amortise the setup of movable type. Inkjet printers achieve continuous-feel production at a single-document scale because the metal type is replaced by *information* (where to put each dot of ink) and the printhead is general-purpose.
- The first requirement (flexible hardware) already exists across industries: industrial robots since Unimate (GM, 1961), CNC milling machines with built-in tool changers, automated machining cells. The bottleneck is the second requirement, which lags badly.
- Existing automated systems react narrowly. A mechanical corn picker filters corn out of a mechanism rather than locating it. Statistical process control adjusts known parameters within set tolerances. Welding robots execute paths a human authored; they cannot decide what to weld. The current state of automation requires a human to specify *what* the process should be.
- **The 3D-printer-replacement-part anecdote.** Engineer Heath Raftery (2023) tried to 3D-print a tent connector. Many design iterations, eventual partial failure under load. Quote: "lurking within some forgettable $50 Kmart capitalist wonder is the carefully applied expertise of designers and engineers." Hundreds of hours spent recreating a mass-produced part. Illustrates that physical-production technology cannot beat scale until the *design-and-figure-out* labour also gets cheap.
- Software simulation is the partial workaround. [Computational fluid dynamics] replaced wind tunnels. Crash testing now starts in silicon. Operations research can simulate factory layouts. Each shrinks the design-feedback loop by replacing real-world feedback with cheap simulated feedback.
- **The simulation ceiling.** Fully simulating the Navier-Stokes equations for an aircraft requires ~20 orders of magnitude more computing power than year-2000 levels. Estimates put the date past 2080 if Moore's law continues. The [smoothness problem] (one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems) leaves open whether Navier-Stokes can represent real fluid behaviour at all. [Chaos theory] (the butterfly effect) caps simulation accuracy for any system with sensitive dependence on initial conditions.
- **Hypothetical: home construction in 24 hours.** John buys a parcel. Software pulls topography, soil, climate, code rules, his family's lifestyle, optionally a preference vector from social media. Generates millions of options, narrows via algorithmic optimisation and high-fidelity family-traffic simulation, lets John tweak. Submits the design.
- **Construction execution in the hypothetical.** Automated foundation truck installs helical piles. Humanoid-robot crew arrives. A truck-mounted sheet-bending rig fabricates steel beams, joists, trusses, and [triple-sandwich panels] (steel/foam/steel/cavity/steel) on demand. Robots weld each into place. Window/door/finish-panel trucks deliver, robots install. Sensors and software inspect continuously; jurisdiction software signs off automatically. Total: <24h.
- The case study's claim is not predictive but illustrative. It shows that *even a highly variable, uncontrolled production environment* can use continuous-process methods if you have sufficiently flexible equipment plus information-processing that figures out the sequence. Many of the primitives (helical piles, sandwich panels, generative design, humanoid robots) already exist.
- **Other resistant industries get the same treatment.** AI plus biological simulation may compress drug-development trial cost. Adaptive curriculum software may compress education time-to-mastery. The pattern: push the upstream design and decision process into computation so the downstream physical process can run continuously and flexibly.
- The bottleneck is now generality. Game-playing AI, self-driving cars, and large language models each demonstrate flexible action in narrow domains. Coupling general-purpose information processing to general-purpose physical automation (humanoid robotics) is the open frontier.
- Caveats Potter names. Cost of land can be 80% of a home's price in cities like San Francisco. Permitting and inspection systems are not yet computer-interpretable. Building codes are prescriptive, not performance-based (Ch 10). Trade unions historically resist (1561 Nuremberg, 1579 Danzig, modern plasterers, plumbers, dockworkers). The new technology is necessary but far from sufficient.
- **The closing argument: efficiency is not inevitable.** The lines trending upward on productivity graphs are the product of deliberate effort by workers, engineers, factory managers, line supervisors. Treating efficiency as a historical force or a policy artefact misreads the mechanism. The book's *progress agenda* is to study how efficiency improvement happens precisely so more of it can be made to happen.
